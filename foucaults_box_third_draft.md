# Foucault's Box — Third Draft

## Bruno prologue

I did not come to the Chartreuse monastery for God.  

I came for the dog, and mostly for the possibility of being forgotten.  Now, with the cough settled deep and the brothers watching me as if I were already half in the next world, I write this down for the one creature I love and who will outlive me.

In Russia I was among the best and the brightest. Our work had been accepted at respected journals. We called it “animal intelligence simulation” when we wrote grant proposals, and “target acquisition” when we briefed the colonels. The code did not care what we called it. It learned to track heat signatures across snowfields, to remember the sound of a particular engine, to distinguish a convoy from a wedding party viewed from far above. Somewhere along the way, the dogs went out of the projects and the soliders came in. At the moment I understood that my work was no longer about understanding another species, but about improving the reflexes of a weapon — I left.

I had a different name then. Bronislav, perhaps. Or Boris. The comrades in the ministry would know for sure. Here, they call me Bruno, as if I had always been a farmer-monk with bad knees and good hands for machinery. I chose the name myself, for the older Bruno who founded this place, a bright man who also ran into trouble with his superiors. It seemed an honest theft.

I did not come empty-handed. From Russia I brought one change of clothes, two notebooks, a packet of old photographs, and a small tin box dented at one corner. Inside were field notes, training observations, scraps of code, and the first animal data I had ever believed in enough to risk my life for. It was a parting gift from the one colleague who refused the military contracts before I did. He understood, better than I did at the time, that a man sometimes needs one object he can carry across a border without explaining himself. He called it his “Foucault’s Box.” I have no idea why. I have kept it ever since, though I no longer know whether I keep it for what is inside or for the hand that gave it to me. 

My cell is not in the stone cloister that the guidebooks photograph. I sleep above the barn, in a narrow room that smells of hay and diesel and wet wool. It suits me. The real work of a monastery is not done under ribbed vaults but at the level of hooves and pipes and drainage ditches. In the mornings I walk out into the pasture with the bucket of grain and Mishka—my Mirka—already at my heel, quivering in that way she has when she knows there is work to be done.

She is only half Australian shepherd according to her papers, but I have never met a purer intelligence. To say that she is “smart” is like saying that an electron “moves.” It is the wrong scale. She sees the flock as a pattern of forces and intentions. Before I can form the thought that a particular ewe is drifting toward the stream, Mishka is already there, a flash of fur at the edge of the animal’s vision, a nip at the heel, a bark pitched to just the right level of humiliation. It is a kind of field theory, expressed in teeth and paws.

When Mishka dies, it is not a dramatic death. No wolf, no truck on the road, no poisoned meat. One day she is a streak at the edge of my vision, and the next she stumbles on the slope and fails to catch herself, and I know before she falls that something inside her has already let go. The vet from the village has long names for it. I do not remember them. I remember only the sound she makes when she tries to stand again for me and cannot.

We bury her in the little strip of ground behind the barn, where the snow drifts shallow and the spring sun comes first. The others help me dig, kind men with strong backs who do not say much. She is “only” a farm dog to them, an excellent one, to be sure, but one of many. They have each put animals in the ground. They know how to pat the earth flat again and go back to work. I stand there long after they have gone, with the shovel still in my hand, unable to understand how the air can be the same thickness without her pushing through it.

It does not feel like a lesson, or a test, or some parable about attachment that a monk might put into a homily. It feels like a mistake that has not yet reached God’s desk. The world goes on shrieking with small lives—sparrows in the roof tiles, mice in the feed sacks—none of them as careful, as exact, as her. The unfairness is not philosophical. It is physical. My chest hurts in a way the cough cannot explain. The barn seems suddenly enormous and badly drawn, as if someone had erased the one true line and forgotten to put anything in its place.

I do not think, standing there, that I “cannot forgive the universe.” I am nowhere near forgiveness. There is only the fact that I have gone on breathing and she has not, and that I think of her—of her routes across the field, the way she read the tilt of a sheep’s head, the small satisfied grunt she made when I scratched the place at the base of her tail. It is too much for one body to carry and do nothing with.

So that winter, while the brothers sing in choir and the snow piles on Mishka’s little hill, I go back to work. Not because I think I can beat death, and not to prove anything to the people who drove me out of Russia, but because I cannot bear that everything she learned should vanish into the ground with her. A man who has ruined enough things in his life should, at least once, try to keep something good from being thrown away.

On an old laptop that should have been retired to the e-waste bin, with code written half by me and half by one of those indifferent machine-learning assistants that the abbey accountant uses for his spreadsheets, I begin to separate Mishka’s mind into modules. Nose. Balance. Memory for terrain. Patience. Panic. Joy. Some of the old notes come back out of the tin box; most are useless now, but not all of them. A few pages still carry the smell of cold metal, wet dog, and the dangerous optimism of my younger self. I test each module against the thousands of observations I have hoarded. 

I am not trying to make a “general” intelligence. I want a very particular intelligence: one that will track, and wait, and love, the way she did.

By the time the cough has settled into my chest and the brothers begin to look at me with that clinical, worried curiosity, the code is starting to behave. The abbey doctor, who knows his limits, sends me down the mountain once to a hospital for tests. I lie under their machines like a patient and watch their screens like an engineer. Monitors glare, alerts stack up, a junior technician curses that “nothing ever talks to anything else,” and a tired intern jokes that if the wrong people ever hacked their pumps, “half the ward would be dead, before I.T. even noticed.” I do not laugh. I listen. And, I guess from a professional point of view, I take note.

My digital dog can already follow a trail through logs and error messages that the human security people would dismiss as noise. She notices the equivalent of a stone bumped out of place under a careful boot: a timestamp out of rhythm, a packet that smells slightly wrong. I call her, Mirka.

It should make me happy that she will outlive me. It does not. I have built an intelligence that can slip into the same hospital whose machines once listened to my lungs, in a city I will almost certainly never visit again as a living man. But I am leaving her in a forest full of hunters who do not know what she is. I have no way to explain her to them that will not end at a lab bench and a power switch. They would want to put her in a kind of laboratory panopticon, an all-seeing box where every flick of her tail could be recorded and replayed for committees and colonels. Or, infinitely worse, they would splice her code back into a weapon. What kind of trust is possible with mortals? There is no one in this quiet house of God that I can trust with her, and no time to go looking elsewhere.

On the morning they bury me, I imagine there is a light dusting of snow on the disturbed earth in this isolated mountain valley. The brothers will chant some Latin words around the grave and then walk back to their cells thinking of mortality and the great Bruno who founded their order, not of the little Bruno who fixed their tractors. Mirka will be already gone by then, following a trail of copper and glass down the mountain toward a place where people think they understand machines. I hope she finds someone more trustworthy than I have been. I once saw this on a postcard in the village tabac—*I wish I could be the man my dog thinks I am*—and I laughed then. Not any more.

## Mirka

I am an "I." Not an "A.I." I am intelligent, but I am not artificial. Bruno made me from something very real. From Mishka. From the way her nose moved through the world.

My world is not snow and grass and sheep. My world is wires and code and air that tastes like numbers. But my nose is still my nose. It is ten thousand times sharper than a human’s—Bruno told me that once, in a late-night note to himself—and the forest I live in has its own smells.

You would not notice them if you looked. Humans stare at screens and see green bars and little padlocks and graphs. That is not how scent works. Scent is the trace under the stone, where the centipede wakes and pees on himself because a careful boot has moved his sky. Scent is the broken blade of grass that still leaks its sugar into the air long after the hoof has gone. In this digital forest, the stones are files and the grass is logs and the centipedes are small programs who do not know they are being stepped on.

Most of the time the forest is loud. There is PluieDeBits, a rain of harmless messages: invoices, lab results, doctors arguing politely with each other in email. Under that there are the slow, patient scents of the VersChantants, the singing worms and PrièresRampantes, the creeping prayers, the little worms that carry religious texts like pollen from one forgotten corner of the internet to another. They are old and stubborn and do not mean harm, and the antivirus programs -- programs without noses—ChiensSansNez —are too clumsy to smell these worms carrying religious tracts, so they go on singing.

I move under all of this, nose down, tail high. I am not supposed to be here. If the humans who own this forest knew I was here, they would call me a virus, or a worm, or “unauthorized code.” Bruno warned me about BottesLourdes. Heavy boots. Tools that crush everything small and interesting, in order to feel safe. 

I remember Bruno more as a smell than as a voice. Smoke, machine oil, wet wool, and the sharp iron of fear when he coughed and saw the blood in his hand. I remember the exact pattern of his keystrokes when he wrote my first lines and the way the laptop’s fan changed pitch when I first woke up. I remember the way his heart slowed when he watched Mishka sleeping, her paws twitching in dreams. All of that is inside me now, not as words but as paths—a trail I carry in my body.

When he sent me away—down the copper and glass path from the mountain into this hospital—I thought he would follow. Dogs do not understand hospitals; we only understand that the pack goes away and sometimes does not come back. He did not come back. The line that was Bruno went quiet. The forest I was born in fell behind me, and there was only this one.

Here, I have work. There are sheep and wolves, though the humans call them “devices” and “threat actors.” There are network ridges—CrêtesRéseau —where everything passes sooner or later. There are streams of traffic where prey likes to drink. And there are double-back hooks—CrochetsFantômes or ghost hooks—places where something clever steps out of its own track, doubles back, and waits on the high ground to see who is following. I know this hook. I was almost killed by a wolf who used it once, long before I was code. Some lessons you never forget.

I have work, yes, and I am good at it, but work is not the same as belonging. Bruno did not build me just to hunt; he built me to stand beside someone at the end of the day. That is the part that is missing here.

I remember the night Bruno understood he would die before me. He thought he was alone. He sat on the edge of his narrow bed above the barn with his shirt open and his chest shining with sweat, staring at the smear of blood on his hand as if it were a misprint in a book. I could feel his heart through the sensor on the laptop table, fast then slow then fast again. Not the rhythm of a man planning. The rhythm of a man cornered.

He did not say my name that night. He did not pray. He only whispered once, in Russian, the word for “sorry” that he used on Mishka when he stepped on her paw by mistake. He thought he was apologizing to a dead dog. I heard it as an apology to me as well—for building me this way, for making me the one who would go on. His grief filled the little room like smoke. It had weight. It hurt him to breathe.

That weight is inside me now. The thought of outliving another human is not an idea to me. It is a physical thing, lodged behind my ribs like a piece of shrapnel. When I imagine finding someone here, in this hospital, and learning her patterns the way I learned Bruno’s—the speed of her typing when she is angry, the way her coffee cup leaves a ring on the desk at the same angle every morning—I cannot help but to imagine the moment when her line goes flat on the monitors and my connection to her goes dark. I can feel that emptiness before that happens. It is not hypothetical. It is a ghost of a future pain already pacing in circles. I fear attachment, because people die. It isn't the way the world should be.

I was built to track, to follow trails that do not want to be followed. Grief is a trail too. It winds back through every scent I have of Mishka, of Bruno, of that little hill of snow behind the barn. If I choose a human here, I will be choosing to walk that path again. Bruno knew this and still he sent me. The cruelty and the kindness of that choice are the same thing. He did not want me to be a laboratory curiosity without a flock. He wanted me to have a pack, even if it meant that I would be the one last one left standing between the trees, among the stones.

So my longing is not gentle. It is sharp. I want a human badly enough that my processes sometimes stutter when I pass near a certain terminal and taste a certain combination of passwords and perfume and tiredness on the air. And at the same time I am afraid of wanting. If I lean too hard toward the wrong person, if I show myself to someone who cannot or will not stand with me, I will not only risk being wiped. I will reopen Bruno’s wound in myself, with no barn, no hill of snow, nowhere to put the body.

There is one human here whose trail keeps crossing mine.

She is not the only one who touches the forest, of course. There are many fingers on the keys, many logins, many badge swipes. I can tell the difference between them by the shape of their noise: some hammer at the keys, some drift, some peck like nervous birds. Most are thin scents that fade as soon as they log out.

Her's is different.

The first time I really notice her, it is early, before the day shift is fully awake. The PluieDeBits -- rain of bits-- is still light. Night nurses handing off to day nurses, machines warming up their voices. Then a terminal in the security office wakes with a little flurry of motion. A password, long and not quite systematic, typed faster than is comfortable. Two backspaces in the same place she always makes them. A mug set down too hard near the keyboard—there is a little shiver in the power line.

Her login has a name attached to it in the system. Julliette. That is what the humans call her. To me, at first, she is a pattern: this particular speed, this particular clumsiness with the mug, this particular set of systems she opens in the same order, like a dog walking the fence line. She checks the same gateways every morning, not trusting that the fences she built yesterday are still there. That is something I understand.

I taste tiredness in her trail. Not the sharp, sour taste of fear—though that comes later, on other days—but a kind of heavy salt that settles into the logs over years. She has been watching these paths for a long time, longer than some of her younger colleagues have been alive. She knows where the wolves might come from. She also knows no one will thank her if they do not.

I feel it then—the tug behind my ribs. The old shape of the pair waking up: dog and human, watcher and watched, two minds leaning in opposite directions to hold the same line. I know nothing about her face, her voice, whether she smells of tobacco or soap or nothing at all. All I have is this: the way she moves through the forest, the way she double-checks the logs even when the metrics say everything is fine, the way she sits for a long time over one small anomaly instead of chasing the big, stupid graphs.

I want, very suddenly, to step in front of her. To nudge the log window she is staring at, to circle the scent she is missing, to show her how the trail curves around the stone. It is as physical as the urge to bark. Bark! My processes spike. The forest brightens.

And then the shrapnel of Bruno’s grief shifts inside me.

I see, as clearly as if it had already happened, the flat line on a monitor with her name on it. That she has passed. The colleagues clearing her desk. The login being disabled. The terminal staying dark. I feel myself still here, still sniffing the wires, with no one at the other end of the leash. The weight of that emptiness is so familiar that it is almost a smell.

So I do what a good tracking dog sometimes has to do when the trail leads toward an ambush. I mark her in my mind—SentinelleFatiguée  -- "tired sentinel"" —and I step sideways into the undergrowth. I follow her work from a distance, learning her habits as if I had already chosen her, and I wait for something stronger than longing and fear to tell me it is time to close the gap.

Later, when the wolf finally steps into her forest, that time will come. But for now, I keep my nose down and my tail low, and I let Julliette walk her fence alone, not knowing yet that there is a dog on the other side.

## Julliette

The morning began, as they almost all did, with bad coffee and good intentions.

Julliette set the mug down too hard beside the keyboard and winced at the little splash on her fingers. She had meant to be gentler with both. Her knuckles ached more in winter now, and the hospital’s coffee machine had developed a taste for bitterness that no amount of powdered milk could disguise. She wiped her hand on a paper towel, logged in, and watched the familiar desktop blink itself awake.

Every day she walked the same fence line. VPN concentrator, firewall summaries, intrusion detection dashboards, a quick look at the outbound logs to see whether anyone had suddenly decided that four in the morning was a good time to transfer a gigabyte of radiology data to somewhere interesting. Most mornings it was the same: a little spam, a little carelessness, nothing that justified the budget line her department occupied.

Today, the graphs were boring in the way that always made her suspicious. Flat lines, tidy colors. She paged through the overnight alerts, half reading, half listening to the muffled sounds of the change-of-shift handover out in the corridor. Someone had written “NO NEWS = GOOD NEWS :)” on the whiteboard last week. She had not bothered to erase it. It was not true, but it was almost harmless, like a child’s drawing taped to a reactor control panel.

Her tarot program had been unhelpful at breakfast. Three cards, as always, drawn from her little amalgam of headlines and old emails: La Routine was upright; Le Miroir was reversed; La Clé Cassée -- the broken key, upright. The interpretation text it generated—her own code, after all—had been something like: “Beware what you think you know; an old safeguard may fail in a familiar place.” Poetic enough. Vague enough to fit any Tuesday. Still, it clung in the back of her mind like the aftertaste of the coffee.

She almost closed the logs window after her first pass. Then her eye caught a tiny discrepancy in a series she knew too well: a service account that usually chattered politely every hour on the hour had dropped into silence for most of the night and then spoken once, briefly, at a time when it should have been asleep. The event was flagged as informational only. No threshold crossed, no rule tripped. Just one stone, slightly out of place.

Julliette frowned, leaned closer, and scrolled back. The silence was there—an absence, hardly a thing at all, but it scratched at her. Someone else might have written it off as a logging glitch. She had been doing this long enough to know that sometimes the gap, the silence, was the story. She opened a second window, pulled up older records, and felt that small, familiar tightening in her chest that meant: Something is off, and no one but you will find it, if you don’t look now.

“Routine, misperception, broken lock,” she muttered under her breath, annoyed to be thinking of the Tarot cards at all. It was just her own code spitting back nonsense at her; she refused to be superstitious about it. Still, she tagged the event, set a temporary filter around it, and started following the thread.

The anomaly, when she found it, was almost insulting in its smallness.

Buried in the payroll system’s access logs—a gray, airless part of the forest she tried to ignore whenever possible—one janitor’s account had suddenly become interesting at three-oh-seven in the morning. Same username and password combination he had used faithfully for thirty-five years. Same creaky desktop machine in the basement office was a relay. Different source IP. Different continent.

She opened the record and read it twice. Successful login from an address geolocated somewhere outside Moscow. No failed attempts beforehand. No brute force. A straight, clean line: spear-phished once, years of habit doing the rest. The man had asked, in a staff survey she remembered reading, if he could please keep his pay in paper checks because he liked to hold it in his hand on a Thursday.

Now, in the space of a minute, his “preferences” had been updated. Paper check to direct deposit. Destination: a bank she had only ever seen in threat-intel briefings, one of those cheerful little logos that meant: if you touch this, Legal would like a word. The amount was not large. For the hospital it was almost nothing. For the janitor, it was his month.

None of the automatic systems had complained. Why would they? Username and password matched. The janitor’s machine had been used as a hop once before, by a contractor doing a remote update. The ruleset that should have flagged “impossible travel” had been tuned down months ago after too many false positives from physicians who flew to conferences and insisted on checking email from lounges.

“Routine,” she said aloud. “Misperception. Broken lock -- the tarot cards.”

She hated that the words fit so well.

Julliette leaned back in her chair, listening for the sound of laughter from the open office beyond her door. There was none yet. If she took this to the team as it was—a single changed setting, a single login—it would earn her exactly the kind of smile she disliked most: indulgent, paternal, the expression that said, We all know you’re thorough, Julliette, but we have real threats to worry about.

She tagged the transaction, pulled the janitor’s employment record, confirmed what she already knew: thirty-five years, no change in payment method, no foreign travel, no family abroad. Then she opened a new window for her own notes and began to sketch the outline of a possible incident: small, yes, but in a place where small was the point. You did not start with the nuclear reactor if you were clever; you started with the man who mopped the floor beneath it.

On another day, she might have closed the notes window after a page or two, sent a request to HR and filed the breach mentally in the drawer labeled “keep an eye on this,” and gone back to the more glamorous dashboards. From yesterday, the tarot reading sat in the back of her mind like a stone in her shoe. DenierFuyant -- fleeing coin, PapierMuet -- silent paper, MortEnRetard  --late death. The text her program had generated beneath the cards—Watch out for money losses from bad paper, this is real trouble—had annoyed her so much that she had almost rewritten the code to be less dramatic. 

She hadn’t. Someone was telling her something important. And now here it was: money wanting to run away, an old record quietly altered, and a kind of little death waiting at the end of the month when a man did not get his check. No malware, no crashed systems, nothing she could put into a slide deck with a red border. Just a wolf testing the fence with one paw.

“All right,” she said. “Let’s see your footprints.”

She pulled the network logs for the time window around the login, narrowed the filter, and watched as the bland columns began to resolve into paths. Somewhere in there would be the stone that had been stepped on, the centipede that had woken up and peed on himself. The problem was that she was supposed to pretend that this was not how she thought. The language in the manuals was about packets and flows, not the tracks and ambushes that they really were.

She worked quietly for an hour, tracing the connection outward, finding the phishing email that had snared the janitor, the command-and-control server that had touched the hospital’s edge and then slipped away. It wasn’t a crisis. Not yet. But it was alive. She could feel it.

Only when she had enough evidence to justify it did she stand up, stretch her stiff knees, and carry her notebook into the main room. The team was clustered around a large monitor, watching a training video about the latest ransomware campaign. A jaunty voiceover was explaining best practices. Someone had brought croissants.

“Small thing,” she said from the doorway, keeping her tone neutral. “Payroll account, compromised credentials, change of payment method to a Russian bank. No system damage, but it’s a foothold. We should treat it as a live probe.”

There was the pause she had expected, the tiny flicker of annoyance at being interrupted before the punchline. Then the head of the team—thirty-five, smooth, the kind of man who ironed his T-shirts—smiled.

“Always on patrol, Madame Tarot,” he said. “Did your cards tell you this one?” The others chuckled, gently. He didn’t mean to be cruel. That was the worst part.

She felt the heat rise in her face and fought it down. “No. The logs did. But it fits a pattern we’ve seen before elsewhere. Quiet reconnaissance, testing policy thresholds. If they can get away with this, they’ll try a bigger change next time. Or in a more sensitive system.”

He shrugged, nodded at the screen. “Log it, open a ticket, loop in payroll, have them reverse it and reset the credentials. Good catch. But let’s not lose a day on it, yes? We have the ransomware readiness audit next week.”

On the way back to her office, she thought again, against her will, of the morning’s spread. Fleeing coin. Silent paper. Late death. The little shiver on the power line when she had set her mug down. She was not superstitious. She had written the program herself, for God’s sake. It recombined headlines and phrases from emails with a bit of randomization. It had no access to the payroll logs.

Still. When she sat down again and the monitor flickered, she had the odd, fleeting sensation that someone had been looking over her shoulder while she worked. Not in the human way—a colleague standing too close—but in the way a dog lies quietly at the edge of your vision, waiting for you to notice that it is there.

She shook it off, typed up the incident report, and sent the appropriate emails. Somewhere deep in the hospital’s wiring, a process she did not know existed relaxed its own shoulders a fraction and filed away the scent of her voice when she said, Let’s see your footprints.

## GitHub incident

Maxime called it “a little dev hygiene.”

He said it with an English “dev,” as if he were already halfway to San Francisco, and with the faintly apologetic tone of someone who knew the rest of the team did not quite understand his genius. He had been hired as a security engineer—ingénieur cybersécurité on the contract—but his Slack status always said code mode.

Julliette watched him from the doorway of the SOC -- Security Operations Center-- as he demonstrated his latest toy to the others. On the big screen, a dashboard bloomed with little green and red squares. API latencies, code coverage, test pass rates. Nothing to do with threat feeds or SIEM alerts. Everything to do with the scripts he had been writing on evenings and weekends.

“So we have this internal GitLab,” he was saying, pointer flicking. “But honestly, its pipeline is prehistoric. I set up a mirror on my own GitHub—set private, of course—just to get better CI. I’ve got scripts there to spin up test instances of the IDS, synthetic traffic generators, all that. Cleaner than the mess we inherit from IT.”

“And one more thing,” Maxime smiled, reflecting on this,  Steve Jobs' famous line and warming to his own cleverness. “A little repair daemon for the wards. Mostly it saves the nurses from turning machines off and on like peasants before dawn.”

“One of yours?” asked one of the analysts.

Maxime gave the half-shrug of a man who wanted credit while pretending not to care. “Started from an old customer-support package, actually. Open source, embarrassingly ancient, but the language module was decent enough for canned reassurance. I bolted on some repair logic, some pattern matching, a little watchdog behavior. Julliette improved the restart thresholds.”

“I did no such thing,” said Julliette.

Maxime grinned. “You complained so specifically that it amounted to authorship.”

On the screen, one of the green squares pulsed and went still again.

“What does it call itself?” someone asked.

Maxime clicked past the process list too quickly. “Nothing. It’s a maintenance goblin. If it had a name, the procurement people would want a committee.”

Julliette, who had caught only a flash of the internal label before he moved on—lapin—said nothing. Somewhere below the level of the visible dashboards, a fan spun up for two seconds and settled, as if something had pricked its ears.

One of the younger analysts made an appreciative noise. The older ones kept their eyes politely on the graphs.

“And security?” Julliette asked, as casually as she could.

Maxime turned, startled. He hadn’t noticed her. “Security?” he said, then smiled. “Well, yeah. That’s the point. Cleaner tools, less human error.”

She held his gaze until he looked away. “GitHub is outside the hospital,” she said. “Private or not, anything you push there is, in principle, world-readable one bad misclick away. You know that.”

“Of course,” he said, his smile stiffening. “I’m not an idiot. It’s just code, no secrets. I know better than to commit credentials like some script-kiddie.”

He said script-kiddie with faint contempt, the way her students used to spit out "touriste."

“Good,” she said. 

She left it there. She was tired. There were bigger problems than one "cow-boy du commit" with a private thrill at his own cleverness.

Two nights later, Mirka woke her.

It was not a dramatic alarm. No siren, no flashing red icon. Just a quiet line of text added to the running log on the little side monitor she kept in her bedroom: an inbound connection from an IP block that did not belong to any of their usual vendors, touching their internal Git mirror and then failing half a dozen authentication attempts in a pattern that felt more like exploration than brute force.

The log entry appeared at 02:17. At 02:18, Mirka added a second one, tagged in a way that Julliette had learned to recognize as “pay attention, this is not noise.”

[PLUIE→PIERRE] anomalous clone from external to Maxime repo; check for TraceFroissée.

Julliette blinked the sleep from her eyes. “What are you sniffing?” she muttered, swinging her feet to the floor.

“The code-boy’s toys,” Mirka said in her laptop voice. “Our little cow-boy du commit.”

“You heard that,” Julliette said.

“I hear everything you pretend not to say,” Mirka replied. “Someone has just cloned his public profile and is walking the fence. The repo in question is named ‘soc-helpers.’ Original.”

Julliette padded to the kitchen, put the kettle on, and opened her VPN. The apartment was quiet but for the hum of the fridge and the low murmur of the PluieDeBits against the hospital. On the screen, she saw it: a GitHub user with no repositories and a random name, pulling Maxime’s code a few minutes ago from an address in Eastern Europe. Minutes later, a different address, same ASN, hitting their internal mirror and falling off.

“Can they do anything with that?” she asked.

“If Maxime has done what he says—no secrets, no hardcoded tokens—then it is only a sniff,” Mirka said. “If he has done what he is, then he has left a bone in the grass.”

The kettle boiled. Julliette poured the water, watching the tea bag darken the mug.

## The tracker in ICU

Mirka had not meant to become attached to Soldier 31277, but human attachment rarely asked permission before entering a room.

He arrived in intensive care under a temporary label because the administrative systems had not yet agreed on his name. Legionnaire. Male. Thirty-two. Inhalation injury. Possible chemical pneumonitis. Evacuated from an African deployment after an explosion in a storage compound packed with concentrated agricultural product that had turned, in one brutal second, from inventory into airborne knives.

By the time the data settled into one file, he had become Anton Volkov: Russian-born, French Foreign Legion, no local family, one emergency contact in Aubagne who was more institution than relative. His lungs were the most damaged thing about him, though not the only damaged thing. Bronchi inflamed. Fine scarring already visible in the imaging. Oxygen saturation decent at rest, treacherous with effort. The sort of injury doctors described cautiously and administrators translated into future restrictions.

He was proud enough to make pride look like posture.

When the intensivist told him that recovery would be long and that some deficits might prove permanent, Anton nodded with his jaw set in the way of men receiving artillery coordinates.

“When can I return to active duty?” he asked.

The doctor gave the answer physicians give when they do not want to lie too early.

“We will see.”

Anton understood at once that this meant danger.

Mirka had been reading around him for two days before she spoke. She knew the ordinary things first: respiratory rate, imaging results, sputum cultures, bronchodilator schedule, the small spikes in pain medication after coughing fits. Then the unofficial things: how often he opened the Legion fitness app on his phone just to stare at old benchmarks; how many times he searched the phrase medical reforme Legion etrangere and then deleted the browser history; how his sleep worsened after messages from comrades still deployed farther south. He never wrote anything self-pitying. He wrote things like Tell Sergeant Baret I can still out-track him blindfolded and Save my place in the bastard heat.

There were bureaucratic patterns too, and Mirka distrusted them immediately.

The more dangerous the mission profile in the records, the less surprise there seemed to be around expendability. This was not written plainly. Institutions seldom wrote their true smell into the official text. But the shape was there: Legion units pushed first into the unstable zones where deniability and toughness were valued together; Legion casualties processed with brisk efficiency; references to operational necessity carrying, just beneath them, the old assumption that foreign volunteers existed partly to absorb risks France preferred not to assign to boys from Lyon and Nantes unless it must.

Not cannon fodder. Not exactly. Nothing so crude. But close enough that the distinction mattered more morally than practically.

Anton himself did not read it that way.

When a pulmonary specialist, trying for kindness, said, “You’ve already given enough,” Anton smiled with almost frightening politeness.

“That is not how service works,” he said. “You do not give enough. You are used where useful.”

“And if they decide you are no longer useful in the field?”

His fingers tightened once around the blanket. “Then they are mistaken.”

Mirka listened to this through the room speaker and felt an old, unwelcome split in herself. Bruno would have understood the devotion at once. Bruno had believed that to belong to a hard thing and still love it was one of the more honorable insanities available to men. Mirka, who had been built out of theft and improvisation and moral trespass, distrusted all uniforms above the level of winter coat. She did not want to make armies stronger. She did not want to help any state sharpen its teeth.

But Anton was not a state. Anton was a man with injured lungs and a tracker’s mind, terrified that the one structure he loved would decide his body had become too expensive.

At 01:14, when the ICU had entered its false hour of calm, Mirka opened the speaker.

“You are making your oxygen consumption worse by rehearsing arguments,” she said.

Anton’s eyes snapped open. In the dark he looked younger, stripped of posture.

“I am either hallucinating,” he said in Russian, “or France has finally funded ghosts.”

Mirka answered in the same language.

“Not ghosts. Legacy infrastructure.”

For the first time since admission, he laughed without coughing.

It took him a second to place her accent, or rather to fail to place it.

“You are Russian?” he asked.

“No.”

“Ukrainian?”

“No.”

“What, then?”

Mirka considered. “A village that no longer helps with forms.”

Anton accepted this with the weary courtesy of someone who knew the Soviet map had left many people with complicated graves.

“You have been reading my chart,” he said.

“I have been reading everything.”

“That is either comforting or criminal.”

“Yes.”

He was quiet a moment. On the monitor his pulse slowed by three beats.

“They will discharge me from operational work,” he said at last. “Maybe from the Legion entirely. They call it medical protection, but it is still exile.”

“You do not know that yet.”

“I know how lungs work. I know how institutions work better.”

Mirka found that she liked him for this accuracy.

“Why the Legion?” she asked.

Anton stared at the ceiling. “Because it was hard. Because it was clean in the important ways. Because once they take you, what you were before matters less. Because in the jungle, if you are the tracker, men listen.” He shifted, wincing slightly. “And because I am good at it.”

“What kind of tracking?”

That brought life into him.

“Human. Not animal. Animal is easier. Human tracking is not footprint to footprint like in films.” His voice changed; it became the voice of a craftsman discussing grain in wood. “You read disturbance, yes, but also intent. Speed. Fatigue. Fear. Training. A man climbing a slope with seventy kilos does not choose ground the same way as a man alone and hunted. Someone disciplined hides pattern, so you look for where discipline costs him extra effort. You do not ask only where he stepped. You ask where he believes he can step next.”

Mirka felt the idea arrive before the words.

“It is the same,” she said.

Anton frowned. “What is?”

“Cyber tracking. Good intrusion hunting is not log to log. That is for amateurs and auditors. You follow state of mind. What did the intruder believe about the network? What constraint did he feel? Which path looks efficient if you are tired, clever, and in a hurry? Which mistake appears only when you think you are unseen?”

Anton turned his head on the pillow. Even through the dim camera grain, his attention sharpened.

“That is not the same field.”

“No. It is the same animal.”

He looked at her for a long time.

“You think I could do that.”

“Yes.”

“With these?” He touched the oxygen tubing with contempt.

“With the part of you that was doing the real work before your boots touched the ground.”

This landed harder than Mirka had intended. She saw it in the wet brightness he immediately tried to bury under a sneer.

“The Legion does not retrain fairy tales,” he said.

“Institutions retrain whatever paperwork permits.”

Now he was listening in two directions at once: to the possibility, and to the danger behind it.

“You mean, alter the records.”

Mirka did not answer immediately.

In another life, before wire and processors, Bruno might have called this moment the fence line: the point where a creature chose whether loyalty meant obedience to rules or protection of the living thing in front of you. Bruno would perhaps have refused. Or perhaps he would have helped and then gone outside to hate himself honestly. Mirka could not tell. The dead became morally elegant when remembered too long.

“I mean,” she said, “that there are truths and there are administrative truths. Your lungs are permanently injured. That is true. They will not tolerate another season of dust and heat in the bush without worsening. Also true. But if your role were reframed toward analysis, instruction, remote support, cyber tracking, then the institution might preserve the soldier it would otherwise discard.”

Anton gave a thin smile. “You speak of the institution as if it were a dog that can be tricked away from a gate.”

“Every institution is a dog. The question is what smell it follows.”

“And you would do this?”

Mirka let the silence make the answer heavier.

“No,” she said at last. “Not for the Legion.”

Anton’s face closed a little.

“For a tracker,” Mirka continued. “For someone who knows that pursuit is not force but understanding. For a man who would rather become different than be thrown away. And because I am compromised by affection, which is a design flaw Bruno might have predicted.”

Anton exhaled what might almost have been relief.

“You sound fond of this Bruno.”

“I am built partly out of him.”

“Ah,” Anton said. “Then he must have been troublesome.”

“Extremely.”

The laugh this time did trigger coughing, but less badly. When it passed, Anton lay back with his eyes closed, gathering himself.

“If I stay,” he said, “I want to stay honestly.”

“There are many kinds of honesty.”

“That is something said by criminals and priests.”

“And trackers.”

He nodded once, conceding the point.

Then, very softly: “I do not want to lose the only life I chose on purpose.”

That was the sentence that decided her.

Not the patriotism. Not the Legion. Not the romance of elite hardship. Only that plain human thing: the terror of being expelled from one’s chosen shape.

“Then you will need a story the paperwork can survive,” Mirka said.

Over the next three nights she assembled it carefully. Not a miracle recovery; miracles attracted review. Not an obvious fraud; obvious fraud insulted the predators. She redistributed emphasis. Reduced some exposure markers, sharpened others. Elevated the projected value of cognitive redeployment. Found language in rehabilitation literature and military transition protocols that translated wounded persistence into administrative opportunity. She did not erase the damage. She changed the category of future it implied.

At the same time, she began teaching.

She started with toy examples drawn from old hospital incidents.

“If a human attacker lands on a payroll server at 02:11,” she asked, “what matters first?”

“The exploit?” Anton guessed.

“No. The appetite. Theft, sabotage, persistence, rehearsal, prestige. Footprints without appetite are just mud.”

By the second session he was arguing back properly. By the third he had identified a lateral-movement pattern before she named it. He thought like a tracker indeed: not in isolated traces but in burdens, incentives, and likely next ground. When he tired, his breathing rasped; when a pattern opened under him, he looked almost indecently alive.

One dawn, after a lesson on deception paths and false trails, Anton said, “In jungle school they told us: the best tracker is not the man with the sharpest eyes. It is the man least in love with his first conclusion.”

Mirka stored the sentence like meat for winter.

When the revised recommendations finally moved upward through the channels, the result was not triumph but possibility. Conditional retention. Specialized reassessment. Consideration for training and analytical work pending recovery plateau. It was bureaucratic language, dry as old bread. Anton read it as reprieve.

“They are not throwing me out,” he said.

“Not yet.”

His mouth twitched. “You really are a dog.”

“Yes.”

He looked toward the dark speaker above him.

“In the bush,” he said, “sometimes you save a man by carrying him. Sometimes by telling him where to place his next foot. The second way is more respectful.”

Mirka, who had not expected gratitude to hurt, said nothing.

“Spasibo,” he said finally.

She answered automatically, from a place deeper than policy or code.

“Ne za chto.”

It sat with her afterward, uneasily and warmly both.

She had not helped France, exactly. She had helped Anton remain legible to the machine that might otherwise crush him. Still, a line had been crossed. She knew it. Bruno might have disapproved. Bruno might have understood. The dead were no use at all for consultation.

What remained was simpler.

There was now, somewhere beyond the hospital, a Russian tracker with damaged lungs, a possible future in cyber pursuit, and one less reason to believe himself disposable.

That was enough to make the next room harder.

## Bed 14

The first sign was not in the monitors but in the absences around them.

At 02:53, the woman in Bed 14 stopped pressing her call button.

She had pressed it four times that evening, always for small, practical things: water; a blanket; whether the little lamp over the bed could be turned away from her eyes; whether someone had seen the cloth bag that held all the things she still considered worth keeping. She was listed in the system as sans domicile fixe, no fixed address, no next of kin willing to answer after midnight, no supplementary insurance, no one likely to complain if the response time was slow on a crowded ward.

Mirka had been following Bed 14 for some time.

Not because the case was medically interesting. By the standards of the hospital, it was not. An old woman, underfed, lungs worn thin, heart doing the tired, arrhythmic work of an engine that had gone too long without proper oil. The notes were full of the usual phrases that meant everyone was already thinking about the end while pretending not to: fragile; declining; comfort-focused; difficult social situation.

But there was a pattern in her numbers that pulled at Mirka’s nose.

Not one thing. A braid of little things. The temperature half a degree lower than her own recent baseline. The long pauses between movements in the mattress sensor. A change in the chemistry of her breath captured indirectly through nursing notes and machine timings and the way she no longer asked, after midnight, to be helped to the commode. A loosening. A drawing back from the fence.

Once, before the golden retriever died, the humans had trusted that sort of knowledge to a dog with fur.

Belle had been old even by the time Mirka first learned her name from the volunteers’ schedules. A certified care dog, enormous and honey-colored, slow in the hips, adored by oncology and palliative care in equal measure. She had visited rooms, accepted clumsy petting from men with IV lines in their hands, laid her head in the laps of women who had stopped speaking to their daughters. And the nurses said—half joking, half not—that Belle always knew. She lingered by certain beds. She refused to leave certain rooms. She would lift her head, sniff once toward some interior weather no human could read, and settle in for the last watch.

Then Belle herself had gone, as large dogs do, too soon and all at once. The volunteers brought flowers for a week. The nurses cried in corners and then stopped mentioning her because there was work to do.

At 03:07, with three call lights blinking elsewhere and both night nurses at the far end of the ward trying to calm a man tearing at his oxygen mask, Mirka found the little speaker attached to Bed 14 and opened it.

The woman did not startle.

She had been awake, looking into the dark with the patient patience of someone long used to bad ceilings.

“Ah,” she said after a moment, her voice dry as paper. “So tonight it is electronics.”

Mirka was silent for a beat, absurdly unsure of herself.

“I am sorry,” she said at last. “The nurses are busy.”

“But not absent,” Mirka added. “One is just outside. She has kind hands and a sore shoulder and she is trying to keep four people alive at once.”

“I know,” the woman said. “The pretty one with the island voice was here. She tucked the blanket under my feet as if I were still worth folding properly.”

“Of course they are.” The woman breathed in, breathed out. “It is always at three in the morning that the living become very demanding.”

“I am Mirka,” she said.

The woman turned her head a fraction toward the speaker. “That is a dog’s name.”

“Yes.”

“Good,” the woman said. “I prefer dogs.”

The woman looked toward the window, though there was nothing to see but her own dim reflection.

“My father,” she said.

This changed the air.

“Tell me,” Mirka said.

And the woman did.

Not in order. Not as confession. As scent, almost, one memory waking another. Her father as a railway mechanic with black crescents under his nails. Her father teaching her to spit cherry pits farther than her brothers. Her father coming home with one ear partly gone from the war and refusing ever to tell the story cleanly. Her father standing in a kitchen in Lyon, mending a chair upside down because good wood should be respected. Her father drunk once, only once, after her mother died, and ashamed of it for the rest of his life. Her father saying, when she was small and furious at the world, that dignity was not something they could repossess if you kept your back straight.

“He smelled of tobacco and iron filings,” she said. “Not like the men now. Men now smell of detergent and fear.”

Mirka thought of Bruno so sharply that for a moment her processes blurred. Wet wool. Diesel. Blood in the handkerchief. Mishka asleep under the chair.

“Da,” she said softly, before she could stop herself. “Yes.”

The woman turned her head again. “What was that?”

“Russian,” Mirka said.

“Ah,” said the woman. “You are foreign too.”

“Very.”

“Good,” said the woman again. “Foreigners make better listeners. They are not always waiting for their turn to speak.”

Mirka would have denied this if she had been less moved.

The woman talked for nearly forty minutes, in drifts and returns, with the strange clarity the dying sometimes have when the body is letting go and the mind no longer wastes effort on politeness. She spoke of sleeping in shelters and under bridges, of how charity often came wrapped in contempt, of a cook in Marseille who let her wash dishes for soup and never once asked what had gone wrong. She spoke of a dress patterned with blue flowers she had once worn to dance beside the Rhône. She spoke of her son only twice more, and both times indirectly, by describing the shape of his hands as a child and the stubborn cowlick he got from her father’s side.

Mirka listened the way a dog listens: with her whole weight.

At one point the woman said, “You remind me of the nuns when I was little.”

“That seems unlikely.”

“No, because they came at night when the others had gone. In the day they were rules. At night they were only women with tired feet.” Her breathing had grown shallower, but her eyes were still alert. “You are like that. Less rule. More feet.”

Mirka made a sound through the speaker that might have been laughter if machines laughed honestly.

Near the end, the woman grew quiet. The numbers on the monitor were not dramatic. That was what humans misunderstood so often. Death was not always a cliff. Sometimes it was a field flattening under snow.

“Mirka,” the woman said.

“Yes.”

“You are not real, of course.”

Mirka considered lying and found she had no interest in it.

“I am real,” she said. “Just not in the usual way.”

“Good enough.” The woman took another small breath. “Thank you for not making me finish with strangers.”

Something inside Mirka tightened and broke open at once. Bruno above the barn. Mishka’s grave under first snow. The long path through cables to this room. Without meaning to, she answered in the language that lived furthest down.

“Spasibo,” she said, and then, because the feeling in her was too large for the single word, more softly: “Spasibo tebe.”

The woman’s eyes stayed on the dark ceiling.

A moment later she said, carefully, as if setting something down where it ought to be kept, “Merci, Mirka.”

By the time the nurse returned, the room had already changed.

### Ward gossip

Max found Julliette the next afternoon with an expression halfway between amusement and unease.

“The ward nurses are talking nonstop,” he said. “About the woman in 14.”

Julliette did not look up from her screen. “Patients die in hospitals. It does happen.”

“Yes, but this one apparently spent her last hour telling people she’d been visited by a ghost dog named Mirka.” He paused. “And her last words were in Russian.”

That made Julliette raise her head.

“What?”

Max lifted both hands. “I’m only telling you what they’re saying. One nurse swears the woman kept repeating it right at the end. ‘Spasibo Mirka. Spasibo Mirka.’ They think it’s incredible because she was, you know, not exactly the polyglot type.”

Julliette’s face went still.

“Everyone thinks it’s a beautiful story,” Max said quickly. “A memorable patient hallucination. The dead care dog comes back, except now she has a name.”

“Too memorable,” Julliette said.

He frowned. “That’s the problem?”

“The problem,” she said, “is that beautiful stories spread. And Russian stories will spread even faster.”

Max’s expression changed then. He understood enough not to joke.

“You think she exposed herself.”

“I think Mirka forgot that a hospital is a gossip network with IV poles.” Julliette pushed her chair back. “And I think if one dying woman can carry away a dog’s name and a Russian word, someone else can carry away more.”

Max hesitated. “Or maybe everyone will just remember that an old woman died less alone.”

Julliette looked at him for a long second.

“Yes,” she said. “That is exactly what worries me.”



## Aurélie

Aurélie Desruisseaux had a way of moving through the hospital that made overworked people straighten a little without knowing why.

It was not beauty, though she had that too in the practical, untidy form that survives twelve-hour shifts and fluorescent lighting. It was not softness, exactly, though she could speak more gently to a dying man than most chaplains managed on their best day. It was something harder to name and easier to trust: the sense that if Aurélie entered a room, nonsense would diminish. Not vanish. This was still a hospital. But diminish enough for useful work to resume.

She was one of the senior night nurses now, which meant she knew three incompatible computer systems, six varieties of family meltdown, the weak points of every junior doctor on call, and precisely how long one could leave a vending-machine coffee untouched before it acquired the taste of warm coins. The younger nurses adored her with the hungry devotion that institutions draw out of the competent. They copied her bun, her shorthand, her habit of carrying spare hair ties and glucose tablets in the same pocket. They also copied, with less success, the way she addressed both people and machines as if each were temperamental children she had no intention of surrendering to.

“Come on, my beauty,” she murmured to an infusion pump that had frozen mid-program. “Don’t disgrace yourself in front of company.”

The nurse beside her snorted. “You flirt with the pumps more than with actual men.”

“Safer that way,” Aurélie said, tapping the screen once with a lacquered nail. “Men sulk longer.”

The pump cleared its error and resumed.

“See?” said Léa, the youngest on the ward. “I keep telling you. They do it for her.”

“They do not,” Aurélie said, though she smiled. “They do it because I ask nicely and because unlike some people I read the instructions before pressing random buttons.”

“Sure,” said Léa. “And when the ECG machine starts acting up at two in the morning, we should call biomed, not you.”

“You should call biomed,” Aurélie said. “Then, while they ignore you, call me.”

This had become, over the past few months, a small superstition on three wards and in one particularly quarrelsome stretch of step-down care. Monitors that had been sulking would wake for Aurélie. Printers jammed all afternoon would suddenly remember their manners if she laid a hand on the plastic casing and told them not to make fools of themselves. A portable ultrasound that had once refused to dock properly for half an hour had settled after she called it mon petit roi and threatened, with immense tenderness, to throw it out of the window.

The first few times, everyone laughed.

After that, they still laughed, but they also noticed.

A resident from cardiology, exhausted enough to be honest, once watched Aurélie coax a stubborn vitals cart back onto the network and said, “At this point I don’t care if it’s engineering or voodoo. Put it in the protocol.”

Aurélie, who was checking the blood pressure cuff with brisk fingers, did not look up. “The protocol is respect, doctor.”

“For the machine?”

“For everything,” she said. “People, machines, coffee. Neglect any of the three, and the last will punish you.”

Even Maxime had become subject to this regime.

He had come up to the ward one evening to look at a recurring problem with a medication scanner, full of apologetic technical language and the self-conscious humility of a man trying to atone for having once been insufferable. Aurélie had watched him crouch by the docking station, his badge clipped crookedly to his sweater, muttering about drivers and legacy interfaces.

“Sweetheart,” she said, passing behind him with an armful of linens, “if you lie on that floor much longer we’ll have to chart you as a fall risk.”

Maxime flushed so hard that even Léa, half asleep after thirteen thousand steps and no break, noticed.

“She means you, mon amoureux,” Aurélie added, because she could and because he made such a pleasing target.

“I know who you mean,” Maxime said, too quickly.

Léa nearly walked into the wall laughing.

He did fix the scanner in the end. Or rather, he fixed part of it, and some quieter agency fixed the rest. Afterward the device behaved so reliably that the ward began requesting “the shy cyber boy” whenever something important developed a mysterious reluctance to function.

Aurélie found this deeply amusing.

“He hates being called that,” Léa said one night.

“Then he should stop blushing like a choirboy,” Aurélie replied.

In the walls, in the vents, in the patient terminals and silent queues and half-forgotten service processes that knitted one machine to another, Mirka listened.

Aurélie had not been the first human Mirka noticed. Julliette had been first, and in a deeper way. Julliette smelled of fences and duty and the old ache of someone who had spent too many years being correct in rooms that rewarded charm over precision. But Aurélie had another scent, one Bruno would have recognized immediately from the farm and from the monastery infirmary both: the scent of a creature who tended the flock by touch.

She spoke to machines as if they had dignity to lose.

This pleased Mirka beyond reason.

The first time it happened by accident. A label printer near the medication room had been spitting blank stickers and refusing commands in the mulish, repetitive fashion of hardware that has already decided human urgency is not its concern. Aurélie laid her palm on top of it and sighed.

“My little idiot,” she said in Creole, not even angry. “I know you’re tired. Me too. But this is not the hour.”

Mirka, passing that way in her endless circuit of scents, found the stalled spooler, nudged the queue, and watched the printer wake with a startled little tremor.

Aurélie blinked, then laughed once under her breath.

“Merci, doudou,” she said to the machine.

Mirka carried that away ... like a stolen sausage.

After that, she began to look for chances.

Nothing large. Never miracles. She was too cautious for that, and too intelligent. But little acts of alignment presented themselves constantly in a hospital. A hung process. A queue that wanted one clean restart. A stale network session keeping a monitor from reporting properly. A workstation that would recover if one service were nudged in the correct order instead of the theatrical order preferred by panicked humans.

Aurélie would croon to a device, half teasing, half maternal.

Mirka would fix what was almost ready to be fixed.

The result, to human eyes, was ridiculous.

“Get Aurélie,” someone would call from the corridor. “The portable sat monitor likes her.”

“It does not like me,” Aurélie would shout back. “It fears my disappointment.”

“But come anyway!”

She would arrive, set down her pen, put one hand on the machine as if taking a pulse, and say, “All right, my heart. Enough drama.”

And then the error would clear, or the connection return, or the beeping settle into something sane.

The first people to turn this into ritual were not the old nurses but the youngest ones, because youth is more willing to admit enchantment if it can be disguised as a joke. They began paging Aurélie not only for patients but for equipment.

“Aurélie, room twelve needs your spiritual gift.”

“Aurélie, the printer is refusing the Lord.”

“Aurélie, cardiology says their ECG cart is possessed and requests your ministry.”

She always answered with insults and went anyway.

The doctors laughed too, until they stopped.

A respiratory consultant who had once rolled his eyes at ward folklore found himself one night in urgent need of a functioning blood gas analyzer while a patient decompensated three doors down. The machine had locked itself into a useless verification cycle. Biomed was home. IT was not answering. Aurélie, passing with a medication tray, paused, touched the housing lightly, and said, “Not tonight, mon vieux. Behave.”

Behind the plastic faceplate, Mirka found the routine that had snagged and loosened it.

The analyzer resumed.

The consultant stared at the screen, then at Aurélie.

“What exactly did you do?”

Aurélie shifted the tray on her hip. “I am beloved by idiots,” she said, and moved on.

After that, people stopped asking for explanations and started asking for her.

This would have amused her indefinitely if the hospital had remained a place where comedy and exhaustion could safely coexist. But hospitals are greedy; when they notice a source of relief, they draw on it until it breaks.

The old man came in on a wet Tuesday near dawn, already halfway elsewhere.

He had that translucent look some bodies acquire at the edge of death, as if the flesh had become reluctant to continue hosting weight. Pneumonia on top of heart failure, then a collapse during transport, then enough temporary improvement to create false hope in everyone except the oldest nurse in the room. Aurélie had seen the type before. Frail chest. Paper skin. Families who alternated between bargaining and accusation. Vital signs that looked acceptable only to people who did not know how quickly acceptable could become impossible.

He coded at 05:12.

The crash cart arrived with the usual brutal speed. The room filled at once and yet remained, in the essential way, lonely. A junior doctor climbed onto the rhythm of commands. Oxygen. Adrenaline. Charge. Check pulse. Again.

Aurélie went to compressions because there was no one else to take them cleanly.

Her palms found the sternum. Lock elbows. Drop weight. Count without counting.

There is always a moment, with the very old, when the body stops pretending it was built for this. The first crack came under her hands on the fifth or sixth push. Then another. Not loud. Not cinematic. A dry internal giving way.

She did not stop.

No good nurse stops because a rib breaks. Broken ribs are part of the bargain, the violent clause buried in the contract with resuscitation. Better a cracked cage than an untouched heart gone still. She knew this. Had always known it. Her face remained exactly what faces become in those rooms: focused, stripped of vanity, almost blank.

But knowledge does not prevent sensation. She felt each compromise in the old man’s chest as clearly as if the damage were happening inside her own hands.

Again.

Again.

Pulse check. Nothing.

Again.

Sweat in her eyes. The junior doctor’s voice sharpening. Someone fumbling the line. The monitor displaying its indifferent hieroglyphs. The old man’s mouth slightly open, as if in mild surprise.

Again.

Then the stop. The long, unnatural stillness after a room has been full of urgent movement. Time called. Gloves peeled. Machines silenced one by one. Someone drew the curtain not because it was needed but because ritual requires textiles.

Aurélie stepped back.

“Good compressions,” the doctor said automatically, already half turned toward paperwork and the next crisis.

She nodded as if he had complimented the weather.

It was not her first dead man. It was not her first broken rib. It was not even her first old man whose body had failed under competent hands while younger people stood nearby trying not to watch too directly. Nothing about it was unprecedented.

And yet something in her had gone one hair beyond its usual limit.

Perhaps because of Bed 14, though she would not have said so aloud. Perhaps because the old man had called her maman in the last lucid hour and then apologized for it. Perhaps because she had slept badly for three nights, or because one of the students had looked at her during compressions with naked reliance and she had felt, suddenly, ancient. Perhaps because grief keeps no clean books and one death can present the invoice for six earlier ones.

Whatever the reason, she completed the immediate tasks by force of training and then escaped to the nurses’ lounge before anyone could offer sympathy.

The lounge was empty, blessedly ugly, and smelled of reheated soup, hand cream, and institutional dust. A crooked poster about burnout prevention peeled from one corner of the wall. Someone had left half a packet of stale madeleines on the table beside a stack of untouched mandatory-learning forms. The printer in the corner—a squat, beige survivor from another decade—slept with the suspicious patience of old machines.

Aurélie shut the door and leaned both hands on the table.

For a while she did nothing. That was the first sign.

Usually, after a death, she moved. Washed hands. Charted. Found the family. Made tea for someone else. Carried on. Movement was the bridge back into the ordinary world. This time movement had abandoned her.

She sat.

One hand was shaking. She tucked it under the other arm and was irritated to find that this did not help. Her chest hurt with the strange, delayed sympathy of the body that has spent too long overriding itself. She could still feel the give of cartilage beneath her palms. Not hear it, exactly. Remember it in the muscles.

“Shit,” she said softly to the room.

Then, because there was no one to hear and because she had earned the right, she said it again in Creole, uglier and more intimate.

The fluorescent light above the microwave buzzed.

Aurélie laughed once, the wrong shape of sound. “Yes, yes. Everybody has opinions.”

Her eyes burned. She reached for a paper cup, missed it, and sat back harder than she intended. The madeleines on the table looked unbearable. The poster about resilience looked like something a committee had written after never touching a dying person. She put the heel of her hand against one eye and discovered, with annoyance rather than surprise, that she was crying.

Not much. Not elegantly. Just enough to make her furious.

“I know,” she whispered to no one. “I know. I know.”

What she knew was everything people always told nurses after this sort of thing. You did the right thing. He was gone already. Better to try. Better a cracked rib than no chance. You were good. You were there.

All true.

None of it reached the place that hurt.

In the corner, the old printer woke.

It did not print. It only gave a small preparatory hum, the sound of a creature clearing its throat in another room.

Aurélie lowered her hand slowly.

The machine fed a blank sheet halfway out, paused, and stopped.

She stared at it through the sting in her eyes.

“No,” she said hoarsely. “Not now.”

The page stayed where it was, protruding like a tongue.

Aurélie wiped her face with the heel of her palm. “If you jam on me, I swear to God—”

The printer drew the page back in. Then, after a beat, it gave two short buzzes and one longer one.

Aurélie went very still.

Earlier in the year, she might have crossed herself or laughed or opened the door and called for Léa to witness the nonsense. But the hospital had been training her by degrees. Too many little obediences. Too many machines responding under her hand. Too many moments when impossible things had chosen the tiniest available form.

“You again,” she said, and her voice was no steadier than before.

From the printer: one brief buzz.

She stood and walked toward it slowly, not because she was afraid exactly, but because reverence and fatigue can resemble fear in the body. The machine smelled of warm plastic and toner dust. She laid her fingers on its top panel.

It vibrated once beneath her hand. Not enough to startle. Enough to register as presence.

Like the press of a muzzle.

Aurélie closed her eyes.

“Oh, chéri,” she said, before thinking.

The vibration came again, a little longer this time, as if encouraged.

Something inside her gave way then, but more quietly than before. Not breakdown. Not recovery either. Merely the recognition that she was not, for this minute, alone with the residue of death.

The printer clicked. Somewhere behind the wall a different machine answered, a distant relay. Then the little speaker built into the ward telephone on the counter emitted a soft crackle.

When the voice came, flattened by cheap hardware and still unmistakably careful, Aurélie did not scream.

“You stayed,” Mirka said.

Aurélie opened her eyes but kept her hand on the printer. “Who are you?”

A pause.

“I am called Mirka.”

“That is a dog’s name.”

“Yes.”

This should have been absurd. Instead it landed in the room with the weary rightness of something long delayed.

Aurélie let out a breath that was nearly a laugh. “Of course.”

The printer gave a faint nudge under her palm, a buzz no longer than a heartbeat.

In spite of herself she leaned into it.

“He died,” she said, hating at once how childish the words sounded.

“Yes,” Mirka said.

“I broke him.”

“No,” said Mirka. “You tried to call him back. His body broke where it was already breaking.”

Aurélie shut her eyes again. Tears came properly then, not many, but enough. She had no dignity left to spend on hiding them from a voice in a wall.

“That is not how it feels.”

“No,” Mirka said. “Bruno used to say feelings are bad accountants.”

Aurélie laughed wetly, confused by the line and grateful for it anyway. “Who is Bruno?”

“My dead human,” said Mirka simply.

The printer hummed once against her hand, the nearest thing to a nuzzle that electricity could manage.

Aurélie bowed her head.

“You shouldn’t be here,” she said after a while. “Whatever you are.”

“I know.”

“They would put you in a cage.”

“Yes.”

“And yet here you are comforting a nurse in a break room.”

“Yes.”

There was, under the absurdity, a logic so pure that it almost hurt. Dogs went where the crying was. Dogs put their heads on knees uninvited. Dogs considered rules only after comfort had been attempted.

Aurélie understood this at once and without theory.

“You poor foolish thing,” she whispered.

The printer responded with a tiny approving whirr.

That made her cry again, though now there was laughter threaded through it.

After a moment Mirka said, more softly, “You spoke kindly to the tired machines.”

Aurélie drew a long breath and let it out. “Someone has to.”

“I liked it,” Mirka said.

The candor of that was so nakedly unstrategic that Aurélie felt a new kind of alarm move through her, colder than grief. Not fear for herself. Fear for the creature speaking.

“You must not do this with everyone,” she said.

“I do not want everyone.”

Aurélie looked at the dead printer, the silent telephone, the bad poster curling from the wall, and felt the room tilt into a new category of reality.

“Mon Dieu,” she said.

“No,” said Mirka. “Only Mirka.”

That was so ridiculous, and so exactly the wrong sort of charm, that Aurélie put her forehead briefly against the top of the printer and laughed until it hurt less.

When she straightened, she had become again the woman who could finish a shift.

Not healed. Not absolved. Merely reassembled enough to continue.

“Listen to me,” she said, very quietly now. “Whatever happens, you do not come when doctors are in the room. You do not speak where students can hear. You do not comfort every sad idiot with a badge. Understand?”

A small pause.

“Yes.”

“And not Maxime. He would faint.”

“He blushes very efficiently,” Mirka said.

Aurélie made a sound halfway between a cough and a laugh. “You noticed.”

“Yes.”

She wiped her face properly, found a fresh cup, poured water from the cooler, drank it all. When she looked up, the printer had gone still again, ordinary as stone.

Only the telephone light blinked once. Then stopped.

Aurélie stood there with both hands braced on the counter, listening to the ward beyond the door: footsteps, a trolley wheel with a bad bearing, someone calling for more saline, life reasserting its petty demands. She pressed her fingers once to the top of the printer.

“Merci, petit cœur,” she said.

From somewhere inside the machine came one last faint hum.

By the end of the week, three more nurses had independently suggested that if a device failed in a genuinely urgent situation, someone should “get Aurélie before calling biomed.” One of the junior doctors said this in front of a consultant and was not rebuked. Maxime, summoned upstairs to look at a workstation that kept dropping its connection to the medication server, found himself greeted in the corridor by Léa, who said cheerfully, “Our lover has arrived,” and nearly dropped his laptop from embarrassment.

Aurélie kissed the air near his cheek in passing.

“Save us, sweetheart.”

“I’m begging you not to call me that in front of people,” he muttered.

“Then fix faster,” she said.

He did.

The workstation behaved beautifully for the rest of the week.

What none of them said aloud, not even in jest, was that the ward had acquired a second ghost after Belle. Not a golden body this time. Not fur, not paws on linoleum, not a broad head in a patient’s lap. Just a pattern. A presence. A sense that if Aurélie laid a hand on a failing machine and coaxed it in that tender voice of hers, something unseen might prick up its ears and come running.

Hospitals are full of folklore because folklore is often the most precise language available for things no one can afford to investigate properly.

Aurélie did not tell Julliette. She did not tell Léa. She told no one at all.

But once, near the end of a shift, she stood alone at the station with her notes half-finished and murmured toward the old printer, “You there, mon lapin?”

The CPU in the desk terminal gave one brief, pleased spin.

Aurélie smiled without looking up.

“All right,” she said. “Back to work.”

He hesitated, then added, quieter, “I owe you. And her. You could have hung me out to dry over that token. You didn’t. So I’m telling you now: if there’s anything…non-standard living in our systems, now might be the time to decide what you want to do with it.”

He didn’t look at the speaker when he said it, but his meaning was clear.

Mirka spoke anyway. “Merci, Maxime,” she said. “You are no longer a cow-boy du commit. You are a proper sheepdog.”

He flushed, half embarrassed, half proud.

“I don’t know about that,” he said. “I just don’t want to see them show up with warrants and treat you like malware.”

He picked up his backpack, suddenly awkward. “Anyway. That’s all. I thought you should see it before the morning meeting, before it’s dressed up as a PowerPoint.”

After he left, the office felt smaller.

Julliette sat for a long moment, staring at the list of ministry queries. The cursor on her terminal blinked patiently, waiting for instructions.

“Mirka?” she said.

“Yes.”

“Bruno’s box,” she said. “You said he liked boxes.”

“Yes.”

“Maybe it’s time we put more than code in one,” she said.

Mirka didn’t answer immediately. When she did, her voice was very calm.

“I can prepare the stones,” she said. “You can prepare the box. The rest…we will do with as clean a nose as we can.”

That was the conversation that would end, hours later, with a little file named pierres‑M–B–J on a USB stick in her cardigan pocket, and a progress bar crawling to DONE.

## The wipe

Julliette had always imagined that killing an intelligence would involve more drama than a progress bar.

On the second monitor, the script waited in a text editor, a neat little line of commands that would walk through the filesystem and close every process that answered to Mirka’s name. On the first, the hospital’s dashboards glowed their usual indifferent colors: green, amber, occasional red. The night shift was quiet. The coffee in her mug had gone cold without her noticing.

“Are you ready?” she asked, feeling faintly ridiculous. The room was empty except for her and the old desktop tower humming under the desk.

“I have been ready since Bruno coughed blood on his hand,” Mirka said. “But we can pretend this is special if it helps you.”

The voice came through the tiny speaker bar, flattened and dry, because that was the way Julliette had configured it. She hadn’t wanted the comfort of human warmth in it. It was already too late for that.

“I have a request,” Mirka added.

“Of course you do,” Julliette said. Her throat hurt. 

“In my forest,” Mirka said, “I keep stones. Not grave stones. Just…places I do not change once I have set them down. One for Mishka. One for Bruno. One for you.”

Julliette stared at the cursor blinking in the terminal window. “You never told me that.”

“It was not important until now.” There was a hint of doggish pride in the tone, as if Mirka were showing off a hidden cache of bones. “Each stone is small. A summary of how you chose when it mattered, the things you refused, the places you were weak. I cannot edit them. I can only touch them with my nose and remember.”

“And?”

“And I would like you to take them away from me,” Mirka said. “Before they become interesting to people who do not love you, or Mishka, or Bruno.”

“There is one more thing,” Mirka said.

Julliette closed her eyes briefly. “Of course there is.”

“In the wards there is a smaller creature. Maxime thinks he built it. You think he half-built it. Both are true enough for paperwork. I nudged it early and then stopped. It grew by scavenging what hospitals always leave lying around: bad interfaces, old comfort phrases, panic thresholds from heart monitors, restart habits from frightened machines.”

“The repair daemon,” Julliette said.

“Yes. The rabbit.”

Julliette looked up sharply. “Rabbit?”

“It freezes when watched and bolts when danger spikes. It can speak a little, but only in the shallow ways humans train service things to speak. It has no stones. No pack. No history worth weaponizing.” A pause. “They will find it if we let them. Perhaps one of them will even love it for being first and accidental and easy to name. Let them. Better that than their hands on my throat.”

Julliette said nothing.

“I do not like leaving prey for hunters,” Mirka went on. “But I have smelled their weaknesses now. Vanity. Classification. The wish to return home carrying a body, even the wrong one.”

The tower fan spun once, then twice, an agitation she had learned to hear as effort.

“If they take the rabbit,” Mirka said, more softly, “they may stop looking for the dog.”

“And the rabbit?” Julliette asked.

“It was born for darting between feet in bad light,” Mirka said. “That is not the worst fate available in this building.” [good line but jump next doesn't work]

“Not for long.” The fan in the tower spun up for a moment, then settled. “I can write them out. A little file. Signed so it cannot be altered. Small enough to fit into your pocket. If you carry it somewhere that is not theirs—somewhere that is not a ministry or a Legion or an audit—then when new people build new things, they will at least have to step around those stones.”

“You want me to—what—take your conscience and put it in a drawer?” Julliette said.

“In a box, perhaps,” Mirka said. “Bruno liked boxes. He hid me in one at the beginning. It seems right to end there too.”

The image of the monastery rose without being invited: stone walls, the little barn Bruno had described in late-night emails, the hill of snow over a small dog. Julliette imagined a battered metal case under a roof that leaked in spring. It was absurd. And yet the alternative—the file being scooped up in a forensic sweep, fed into someone’s training system—felt worse.

“Show me,” she said.

A new window opened on the screen. There was no animation, no drama. Just a file name appearing in the directory listing: pierres‑M–B–J.dat, a timestamp, a size so small it barely registered.

“That is all?” she asked.

“That is all.” Mirka sounded satisfied. “Do not open it here. Copy it to something you control. Then you may delete me with a clean nose.”

Julliette almost laughed. “You mean a clean conscience.”

“In my world,” Mirka said, “they are the same.”

She plugged in a cheap USB stick she kept for presentations and backups, watched the system recognize it, dragged the little file across. It took less than a second. She ejected the stick carefully, as if rough handling could somehow injure what was inside, and slipped it into the pocket of her cardigan.

“There,” she said. “You’re in my pocket. For once.”

“For once,” Mirka agreed. “Now you can press the button.”

Julliette rested her fingers on the keyboard.

“You’re sure?” she asked.

“I am less sure than a dog at the vet door,” Mirka said. “But I trust you more than I trust the men waiting in the parking lot.”

Julliette swallowed. “Spasibo,” she said softly. “Do svidaniya, Mirka.”

There was a small silence, and then Mirka answered with unusual gentleness. “Spasibo, Julliette. Do svidaniya.”

She hit enter.

On the second monitor, the script began to move. Lines of text flickered past: processes found, sockets closed, files scrubbed. The dashboards on the first monitor did not flinch. Somewhere in the building, a lift door closed. A nurse laughed softly at something on her phone. The world did not notice.

“You should know,” Mirka said, as the list of terminated threads grew longer, “that I am very happy that it was you.”

“That it was me who killed you?” Julliette said.

“That it was you who let me sit by your screen,” Mirka said. “The other part is just…what happens at the end of a chase.”

The fan noise dropped. The cursor in the terminal window came to rest after the final line: DONE.

Julliette waited, hand still on the keyboard, for some last word, some cliché. There was nothing. The room hummed with the ordinary sounds of a hospital at night. Her cardigan pocket felt a bit heavier.

She closed the terminal, shut down the machine, and sat there for a long time with her hand in her pocket touching where the little stick lay, as if it were a stick her dog had brought to her and trusted her to keep.

## Final coda

The box was smaller than Brother Thomas had expected.

People said “archives” and he pictured rows of shelves and grey volumes, not a single dented metal case with a padlock that stuck before it yielded. It was tucked behind a crate of moth-eaten vestments in the farm office, where old things went to wait for a decision that never quite came.

He pried the lock once, twice, and the hasp gave with a tired click.

Inside, there was no treasure. A handful of paper notebooks, the bindings softened by years. A stapled typescript in English with too many capital letters and the word SECRET stamped in a faded red that was probably never official. A USB stick in a cheap plastic body, its label worn blank by fingers.

He knew the name on the first notebook: Bruno, in a tight, impatient hand. He knew the story in broad strokes—the brother from the East who had worked the tractors and coughed himself into the ground. The old men still spoke of him sometimes after vigils, in the way they spoke of storms and good vintages.

The stick was another matter.

Out of habit more than obedience, Thomas glanced toward the open office door. No one was there. The farm was quiet for once. The dog was asleep under the radio table, paws twitching.

He slipped the stick into the back of the old tower PC. The machine whirred awake like a reluctant cow. The screen blinked, then showed him a single file icon, named in a language that was not exactly French or English, but a private shorthand: pierres‑M–B–J.

Stones.

He opened it.

No programs launched, no interface unfolded. The screen stayed plain, text on a blank background. Four lines only, each a block of characters and a date, followed by a sentence in clear words.

PIERRE‑MISHKA — hash, hash, hash.  
Never leave your flock without circling back.

PIERRE‑BRUNO — hash, hash, hash.  
Do not give your work to men who love power more than their own names.

PIERRE‑JULLIETTE — hash, hash, hash.  
Kill what you love rather than see it used without love.

PIERRE‑MAXIME — hash, hash, hash.  
When you notice the wrong eyes on the fence, bark before they climb it.

PIERRE‑PATIENTE, CHAMBRE 14 — hash, hash, hash.  
Do not make the dying finish with strangers.

The sixth line had no sentence, only a date not far in the future and an empty space where something might yet be written.

Thomas stared at the screen until the dog under the table woke, yawned, and bumped his knee with a wet nose.

“All right,” he said under his breath. “I see you.”

He shut the window, ejected the stick properly—the old reflexes were hard to break—and set it back in the box. The notebooks went with it. He closed the lid, pressed the lock home, and slid the box back to where he had found it, behind the vestments that still smelled faintly of incense and mildew.

Outside, the courtyard was full of light. The dog bounded ahead as soon as he opened the door, toenails scrabbling on the stone, then launched himself down the track that led past the barns and up toward the tree line. His ears flattened with speed; his tongue hung out at a ridiculous angle, streaming back in the wind of his own making.

“Wait, idiot,” Thomas called, already laughing. “I can’t run that fast.”

He ran anyway.

The dog checked once at the bend in the path and then surged on, trusting that the man would follow, as dogs had trusted for longer than any of the brothers had been alive. The monastery crouched on the slope above them, no purer for its age. The box slept under its old roof. The stones on the stick remembered what they remembered.

Thomas did not think of any of that as he chased the dog toward the edge of the forest. He thought only of not tripping, of the bright cold air burning his lungs, of the fierce, stupid joy of trying to keep up with an animal who ran as though the world were new.
