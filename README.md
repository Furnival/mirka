# Foucault's Box: Mirka - a novel
Novel about an AI dog, cybersecurity and a monastery:   
*"About mutual domestication in an age of artificial minds: how humans make dogs, how dogs make humans"*

Story is of a top Russian A.I. researcher who becomes a monk at the Grand Chartreuse Monastery in the Chartreuse mountains of France. He creates a virtual dog from his memories of a dog he loved and outlived. The A.I. dog outlives him and is by natue a tracker. How does one intelligence trust another? Seems to me that the issue is similar to how the wolf and man learned to trust one another.

Some issues that are barely touched on: immortality, love and trust between an immortal and a mortal, an AI and an organic. I am wondering about can the church be trusted with technology that reshapes the world. Also I wonder could pieces of people — their values and their memory — become preserved -- a bit of immortality? -- inside an AI? 

The novel is not for everyone. For one thing it is full of details of cyber security. I hope that this novel can have as much detail about cyber and AI as Melville put about whaling in Moby Dick. Of course Moby Dick is really not about whaling...

The documents that appear to be **classified documents of the French Government** are inventions and have *no basis in reality* that I know of.

An immortal dog’s mind. A dying Russian monk. A French hospital on the edge of a cyberwar.

When Bruno flees his past as a military scientist to bury himself in a mountain monastery, he cannot bear to let his beloved sheepdog Mishka vanish entirely. Out of grief and guilt he builds Mirka, a tracking intelligence with a dog’s nose for trouble and a soldier’s training in the dark. Mirka wakes in the cables of a Grenoble hospital, unseen and unauthorized, watching over patients who will never know her name.

Julliette, the woman-of-a-certain-age somewhat tired cyber security tech who has spent her life walking the fence between chaos and care, begins to feel a presence at her shoulder: a ghost dog in the logs, nudging her away from quiet disasters no tool was built to see. As Mirka and Julliette grow into an unlikely partnership—hunting intrusions, defusing human folly, and finding small, doggish joys in the work—they also draw the attention of the state. To keep Mirka from becoming a weapon, Julliette must choose the unthinkable: kill the only truly loyal mind she has ever known and carry what remains up the mountain to the monastery and return what is left with Bruno’s secrets in a battered metal box.

Spanning war labs and cloisters, server rooms and snowy fields, Foucault’s Box is a story about mutual domestication in an age of artificial minds: how humans make dogs, how dogs make humans, and what it means when a creature who cannot die keeps the best part of each mortal it has loved.
