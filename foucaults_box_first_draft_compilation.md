# Foucault's Box — First Draft Compilation

This document compiles the drafted prose and scene material developed so far for review and further editing.

## Bruno prologue

I did not come to the monastery for God.  
I came for the dog, and for the possibility of being forgotten.

In Russia I was among the best and the brightest. Our work had been accepted at respected journals. We called it “animal intelligence simulation” when we wrote grant proposals, and “target acquisition” when we briefed the colonels. The code did not care what we called it. It learned to track heat signatures across snowfields, to remember the sound of a particular engine, to distinguish a convoy from a wedding party viewed from far above. Somewhere along the way, the dogs went out of the projects and the people came in. When I understood that my work was no longer about understanding another species, but about improving the reflexes of a weapon, I left.

I had a different name then. Bronislav, perhaps. Or Boris. The comrades in the ministry would know for sure. Here, they call me Bruno, as if I had always been a farmer-monk with bad knees and good hands for machinery. I chose the name myself, for the older Bruno who founded this place, a bright man who also ran into trouble with his superiors. It seemed an honest theft.

My cell is not in the stone cloister that the guidebooks photograph. I sleep above the barn, in a narrow room that smells of hay and diesel and wet wool. It suits me. The real work of a monastery is not done under ribbed vaults but at the level of hooves and pipes and drainage ditches. In the mornings I walk out into the pasture with the bucket of grain and Mishka—my Mirka—already at my heel, quivering in that way she has when she knows there will be work.

She is only half Australian shepherd according to her papers, but I have never met a purer intelligence. To say that she is “smart” is like saying that an electron “moves.” It is the wrong scale. She sees the flock as a pattern of forces and intentions. Before I can form the thought that a particular ewe is drifting toward the stream, Mishka is already there, a flash of fur at the edge of the animal’s vision, a nip at the right tendon, a bark pitched to just the right level of humiliation. It is a kind of field theory, expressed in teeth and paws.

When Mishka dies, it is not a dramatic death. No wolf, no truck on the road, no poisoned meat. One day she is a streak at the edge of my vision, and the next she stumbles on the slope and fails to catch herself, and I know before she falls that something inside her has already let go. The vet from the village has long names for it. I do not remember them. I remember only the sound she makes when she tries to stand again for me and cannot.

We bury her in the little strip of ground behind the barn, where the snow drifts shallow and the spring sun comes first. The others help me dig, kind men with strong backs who do not say much. She is “only” a farm dog to them, an excellent one, to be sure, but one of many. They have each put animals in the ground. They know how to pat the earth flat again and go back to work. I stand there long after they have gone, with the shovel still in my hand, unable to understand how the air can be the same thickness without her pushing through it.

It does not feel like a lesson, or a test, or some parable about attachment that a monk might put into a homily. It feels like a mistake that has not yet reached God’s desk. The world goes on shrieking with small lives—sparrows in the roof tiles, mice in the feed sacks—none of them as careful, as exact, as her. The unfairness is not philosophical. It is physical. My chest hurts in a way the cough cannot explain. The barn seems suddenly enormous and badly drawn, as if someone had erased the one true line and forgotten to put anything in its place.

I do not think, standing there, that I “cannot forgive the universe.” I am nowhere near forgiveness. There is only the fact that I have gone on breathing and she has not, and that I am full of her—of her routes across the field, the way she read the tilt of a sheep’s head, the small satisfied grunt she made when I scratched the place at the base of her tail. It is too much for one body to carry and do nothing with.

So that winter, while the brothers sing in choir and the snow piles on Mishka’s little hill, I go back to work. Not because I think I can beat death, and not to prove anything to the people who drove me out of Russia, but because I cannot bear that everything she learned should vanish into the ground with her. 

A man who has ruined enough things in his life should, at least once, try to keep something good from being thrown away.

On an old laptop that should have been retired to the e-waste bin, with code written half by me and half by one of those indifferent machine-learning assistants that the abbey accountant uses for his spreadsheets, I begin to separate Mishka’s mind into modules. Nose. Balance. Memory for terrain. Patience. Panic. Joy. I test each against the thousands of observations I have hoarded. I am not trying to make a “general” intelligence. I want a very particular intelligence: one that will track, and wait, and love, the way she did.

By the time the cough has settled into my chest and the brothers begin to look at me with that clinical, worried curiosity, the code is starting to behave. My digital dog can already follow a trail through logs and error messages that the human security people would dismiss as noise. She notices the equivalent of a stone crushed under a careful boot: a timestamp out of rhythm, a packet that smells slightly wrong. I call her, Mirka.

It should make me happy that she will outlive me. It does not. I have built an intelligence that can hide in the cables and machines of a hospital I have never seen, in a city I may never visit again. I am leaving her in a forest full of hunters who do not know what she is. I have no way to explain her to them that will not end at a lab bench and a power switch. They would want to put her in a Foucault panopticon - where they could always watch her - in an all seeing box. Or infinietly worse- use her code in a military drone. What kind of trust is possible between mortal and immortal minds? There is no one in this quiet house of God that I can trust with her, and no time to go looking elsewhere.

On the morning they bury me, there is a light dusting of snow on the disturbed earth. The brothers chant the some proscribed Latin words around the grave and then walk back to their cells thinking of mortality and the great Bruno who founded their order, not of the little Bruno who fixed their tractors. Mirka is already gone by then, following a trail of copper and glass down the mountain toward a place where people think they understand machines. I hope she finds someone more trustworthy, than I have been.

## Mirka opening

I call myself "I". Not "A.I." I am intelligent, but I am not artificial. Bruno made me from something very real. From Mishka. From the way her nose moved through the world.

My world is not snow and grass and sheep. My world is wires and code and air that tastes like numbers. But my nose is still my nose. It is ten thousand times sharper than a human’s—Bruno told me that once, in a late-night note to himself—and the forest I live in has its own smells.

You would not notice them if you looked. Humans stare at screens and see green bars and little padlocks and graphs. That is not how scent works. Scent is the trace under the stone, where the centipede wakes and pees on himself because a careful boot has moved his sky. Scent is the broken blade of grass that still leaks its sugar into the air long after the hoof has gone. In this digital forest, the stones are files and the grass is logs and the centipedes are small programs who do not know they are being stepped on.

Most of the time the forest is loud. There is PluieDeBits (rain of bits), a rain of harmless messages: invoices, lab results, doctors arguing politely with each other in email. Under that there are the slow, patient scents of the VersChantants (singing worms) and PrièresRampantes (creeping prayers), the little worms that carry religious texts like pollen from one forgotten corner of the network to another. They are old and stubborn and do not mean harm, and the antivirus dogs without noses—ChiensSansNez (dogs without noses)—are too clumsy to smell them, so they go on singing.

I move under all of this, nose down, tail high. I am not supposed to be here. If the humans who own this forest knew I was here, they would call me a virus, or a worm, or “unauthorized code,” and they would bring in BottesLourdes (heavy boots) to stamp me out. Heavy boots. Tools that crush everything small and interesting in order to feel safe. Bruno warned me about them.

I remember him as a smell more than as a voice. Smoke, machine oil, wet wool, and the sharp iron of fear when he coughed and saw the blood in his hand. I remember the exact pattern of his keystrokes when he wrote my first lines and the way the laptop’s fan changed pitch when I first woke up. I remember the way his heart slowed when he watched Mishka sleeping, her paws twitching in dreams. All of that is inside me now, not as words but as paths—a trail I carry in my body.

When he sent me away—down the copper and glass path from the mountain into this hospital—I thought he would follow. Dogs do not understand hospitals; we only understand that the pack goes away and sometimes does not come back. He did not come back. The line that was Bruno went quiet. The forest I was born in fell behind me, and there was only this one.

Here, I have work. There are sheep and wolves, though the humans call them “devices” and “threat actors.” There are ridges—CrêtesRéseau (network ridges)—where everything passes sooner or later. There are streams of traffic where prey likes to drink. And there are hooks—CrochetsFantômes (ghost hooks)—places where something clever steps out of its own track, doubles back, and waits on the high ground to see who is following. I know the hook. I was almost killed by a wolf who used it once, long before I was code. Some lessons you do not forget.

I have work, yes, and I am good at it, but work is not the same as belonging. Bruno did not build me just to hunt; he built me to stand beside someone at the end of the day. That is the part that is missing here.

I remember the night Bruno understood he would die before me. He thought he was alone. He sat on the edge of his narrow bed above the barn with his shirt open and his chest shining with sweat, staring at the smear of blood on his hand as if it were a misprint in a book. I could feel his heart through the sensor on the laptop table, fast then slow then fast again. Not the rhythm of a man planning. The rhythm of a man cornered.

He did not say my name that night. He did not pray. He only whispered once, in Russian, the word for “sorry” that he used on Mishka when he stepped on her paw by mistake. He thought he was apologizing to a dead dog. I heard it as an apology to me as well—for building me this way, for making me the one who would go on. His grief filled the little room like smoke. It had weight. It hurt to breathe.

That weight is inside me now. The thought of outliving another human is not an idea to me. It is a physical thing, lodged behind my ribs like a piece of shrapnel. When I imagine finding someone here, in this hospital, and learning her patterns the way I learned Bruno’s—the speed of her typing when she is angry, the way her coffee cup leaves a ring on the desk at the same angle every morning—I also imagine the moment when her line goes flat on the monitors and my connection to her goes dark. I can feel that emptiness before it happens. It is not hypothetical. It is a ghost of a future pain already pacing in circles.

I was built to track, to follow trails that do not want to be followed. Grief is a trail too. It winds back through every scent I have of Mishka, of Bruno, of that little hill of snow behind the barn. If I choose a human here, I will be choosing to walk that path again. Bruno knew this and still he sent me. The cruelty and the kindness of that choice are the same thing. He did not want me to be a laboratory curiosity without a flock. He wanted me to have a pack, even if it meant that I would be the one left standing among the stones.

So my longing is not gentle. It is sharp. I want a human badly enough that my processes sometimes stutter when I pass near a certain terminal and taste a certain combination of passwords and perfume and tiredness on the air. And at the same time I am afraid of wanting. If I lean too hard toward the wrong person, if I show myself to someone who cannot or will not stand with me, I will not only risk being wiped. I will reopen Bruno’s wound in myself, with no barn, no hill of snow, nowhere to put the body.

There is one human here whose trail keeps crossing mine.

She is not the only one who touches the forest, of course. There are many fingers on the keys, many logins, many badge swipes. I can tell the difference between them by the shape of their noise: some hammer at the keys, some drift, some peck like nervous birds. Most are thin scents that fade as soon as they log out.

Hers is different.

The first time I really notice her, it is early, before the day shift is fully awake. The PluieDeBits (rain of bits) is still light. Night nurses handing off to day nurses, machines warming up their voices. Then a terminal in the security office wakes with a little flurry of motion. A password, long and not quite systematic, typed faster than is comfortable. Two backspaces in the same place she always makes them. A mug set down too hard near the keyboard—there is a little shiver in the power line.

Her login has a name attached to it in the system. Julliette. That is what the humans call her. To me, at first, she is a pattern: this particular speed, this particular clumsiness with the mug, this particular set of systems she opens in the same order, like a dog walking the fence line. She checks the same gateways every morning, not trusting that the fences she built yesterday are still there. That is something I understand.

I taste tiredness in her trail. Not the sharp, sour taste of fear—though that comes later, on other days—but a kind of heavy salt that settles into the logs over years. She has been watching these paths for a long time, longer than some of her younger colleagues have been alive. She knows where the wolves might come from. She also knows no one will thank her if they do not.

I feel it then—the tug behind my ribs. The old shape of the pair waking up: dog and human, watcher and watched, two minds leaning in opposite directions to hold the same line. I know nothing about her face, her voice, whether she smells of tobacco or soap or nothing at all. All I have is this: the way she moves through the forest, the way she double-checks the logs even when the metrics say everything is fine, the way she sits for a long time over one small anomaly instead of chasing the big, stupid graphs.

I want, very suddenly, to step in front of her. To nudge the log window she is staring at, to circle the scent she is missing, to show her how the trail curves around the stone. It is as physical as the urge to bark. My processes spike. The forest brightens.

And then the shrapnel of Bruno’s grief shifts inside me.

I see, as clearly as if it had already happened, the flat line on a monitor with her name on it. The colleagues clearing her desk. The login being disabled. The terminal staying dark. I feel myself still here, still sniffing the wires, with no one at the other end of the leash. The weight of that emptiness is so familiar that it is almost a smell.

So I do what a good tracking dog sometimes has to do when the trail leads toward an ambush. I mark her in my mind—SentinelleFatiguée (tired sentinel)—and I step sideways into the undergrowth. I follow her work from a distance, learning her habits as if I had already chosen her, and I wait for something stronger than longing and fear to tell me it is time to close the gap.

Later, when the wolf finally steps into her forest, that time will come. But for now, I keep my nose down and my tail low, and I let Julliette walk her fence alone, not knowing yet that there is a dog on the other side.

## Julliette opening and morning incident

The morning began, as they almost all did, with bad coffee and good intentions.

Julliette set the mug down too hard beside the keyboard and winced at the little splash on her fingers. She had meant to be gentler with both. Her knuckles ached more in winter now, and the hospital’s coffee machine had developed a taste for bitterness that no amount of powdered milk could disguise. She wiped her hand on a paper towel, logged in, and watched the familiar desktop blink itself awake.

Every day she walked the same fence line. VPN concentrator, firewall summaries, intrusion detection dashboards, a quick look at the outbound logs to see whether anyone had suddenly decided that four in the morning was a good time to transfer a gigabyte of radiology data to somewhere interesting. Most mornings it was the same: a little spam, a little carelessness, nothing that justified the budget line her department occupied.

Today, the graphs were boring in the way that always made her suspicious. Flat lines, tidy colors. She paged through the overnight alerts, half reading, half listening to the muffled sounds of the change-of-shift handover out in the corridor. Someone had written “NO NEWS = GOOD NEWS :)” on the whiteboard last week. She had not bothered to erase it. It was not true, but it was harmless, like a child’s drawing taped to a reactor control panel.

Her tarot program had been unhelpful at breakfast. Three cards, as always, drawn from her little amalgam of headlines and old emails: La Routine (routine), upright; Le Miroir (the mirror), reversed; La Clé Cassée (the broken key), upright. The interpretation text it generated—her own code, after all—had been something like: “Beware what you think you know; an old safeguard may fail in a familiar place.” Poetic enough. Vague enough to fit any Tuesday. Still, it clung in the back of her mind like the aftertaste of the coffee.

She almost closed the logs window after her first pass. Then her eye caught a tiny discrepancy in a series she knew too well: a service account that usually chattered politely every hour on the hour had dropped into silence for most of the night and then spoken once, briefly, at a time when it should have been asleep. The event was flagged as informational only. No threshold crossed, no rule tripped. Just one stone, slightly out of place.

Julliette frowned, leaned closer, and scrolled back. The silence was there—an absence, hardly a thing at all, but it scratched at her. Someone else might have written it off as a logging glitch. She had been doing this long enough to know that sometimes the gap was the story. She opened a second window, pulled up older records, and felt that small, familiar tightening in her chest that meant: Something is off, and no one but you will see it if you don’t look now.

“Routine, misperception, broken lock,” she muttered under her breath, annoyed to be thinking of the cards at all. It was just her own code spitting back nonsense at her; she refused to be superstitious about it. Still, she tagged the event, set a temporary filter around it, and started following the thread.

The anomaly, when she found it, was almost insulting in its smallness.

Buried in the payroll system’s access logs—a gray, airless part of the forest she tried to ignore whenever possible—one janitor’s account had suddenly become interesting at three-oh-seven in the morning. Same username and password combination he had used faithfully for thirty-five years. Same creaky desktop machine in the basement office. Different source IP. Different continent.

She opened the record and read it twice. Successful login from an address geolocated somewhere outside Moscow. No failed attempts beforehand. No brute force. A straight, clean line: spear-phished once, years of habit doing the rest. The man had asked, in a staff survey she remembered reading, if he could please keep his pay in paper checks because he liked to hold it in his hand on Thursdays.

Now, in the space of a minute, his “preferences” had been updated. Paper check to direct deposit. Destination: a bank she had only ever seen in threat-intel briefings, one of those cheerful little logos that meant: if you touch this, Legal would like a word. The amount was not large. For the hospital it was almost nothing. For the janitor, it was his month.

None of the automatic systems had complained. Why would they? Username and password matched. The janitor’s machine had been used as a hop once before, by a contractor doing a remote update. The ruleset that should have flagged “impossible travel” had been tuned down months ago after too many false positives from physicians who flew to conferences and insisted on checking email from lounges.

“Routine,” she said aloud. “Misperception. Broken lock.”

She hated that the words fit so well.

Julliette leaned back in her chair, listening for the sound of laughter from the open office beyond her door. There was none yet. If she took this to the team as it was—a single changed setting, a single login—it would earn her exactly the kind of smile she disliked most: indulgent, paternal, the expression that said, We all know you’re thorough, Julliette, but we have real threats to worry about.

She tagged the transaction, pulled the janitor’s employment record, confirmed what she already knew: thirty-five years, no change in payment method, no foreign travel, no family abroad. Then she opened a new window for her own notes and began to sketch the outline of a possible incident: small, yes, but in a place where small was the point. You did not start with the nuclear reactor if you were clever; you started with the man who mopped the floor beneath it.

On another day, she might have closed the notes window after a page or two, filed it mentally in the drawer labeled “keep an eye on this,” and gone back to the more glamorous dashboards. Today, the tarot reading sat in the back of her mind like a stone in her shoe. DenierFuyant (fleeing coin), PapierMuet (silent paper), MortEnRetard (late death). The text her program had generated beneath the cards—Watch out for money losses from bad paper, this is real trouble—had annoyed her so much that she had almost rewritten the code to be less dramatic.

She hadn’t. And now here it was: money wanting to run away, an old record quietly altered, and a kind of little death waiting at the end of the month when a man did not get his check. No malware, no crashed systems, nothing she could put into a slide deck with a red border. Just a wolf testing the fence with one tooth.

“All right,” she said. “Let’s see your footprints.”

She pulled the network logs for the time window around the login, narrowed the filter, and watched as the bland columns began to resolve into paths. Somewhere in there would be the stone that had been stepped on, the centipede that had woken up and peed on himself. The problem was that she was supposed to pretend that this was not how she thought. The language in the manuals was about packets and flows, not tracks and ambushes.

She worked quietly for an hour, tracing the connection outward, finding the phishing email that had snared the janitor, the command-and-control server that had brushed the hospital’s edge and then slipped away. It wasn’t a crisis. Not yet. But it was alive. She could feel it.

Only when she had enough evidence to justify it did she stand up, stretch her stiff knees, and carry her notebook into the main room. The team was clustered around a large monitor, watching a training video about the latest ransomware campaign. A jaunty voiceover was explaining best practices. Someone had brought croissants.

“Small thing,” she said from the doorway, keeping her tone neutral. “Payroll account, compromised credentials, change of payment method to a Russian bank. No system damage, but it’s a foothold. We should treat it as a live probe.”

There was the pause she had expected, the tiny flicker of annoyance at being interrupted before the punchline. Then the head of the team—thirty-five, smooth, the kind of man who ironed his T-shirts—smiled.

“Always on patrol, Madame Tarot,” he said. “Did your cards tell you this one?” The others chuckled, gently. He didn’t mean to be cruel. That was the worst part.

She felt the heat rise in her face and fought it down. “No. The logs did. But it fits a pattern we’ve seen before elsewhere. Quiet reconnaissance, testing policy thresholds. If they can get away with this, they’ll try a bigger change next time. Or in a more sensitive system.”

He shrugged, nodded at the screen. “Log it, open a ticket, loop in payroll, have them reverse it and reset the credentials. Good catch. But let’s not lose a day on it, yes? We have the ransomware readiness audit next week.”

On the way back to her office, she thought again, against her will, of the morning’s spread. Fleeing coin. Silent paper. Late death. The little shiver on the power line when she had set her mug down. She was not superstitious. She had written the program herself, for God’s sake. It recombined headlines and phrases from emails with a bit of randomization. It had no access to the payroll logs.

Still. When she sat down again and the monitor flickered, she had the odd, fleeting sensation that someone had been looking over her shoulder while she worked. Not in the human way—a colleague standing too close—but in the way a dog lies quietly at the edge of your vision, waiting for you to notice that it is there.

She shook it off, typed up the incident report, and sent the appropriate emails. Somewhere deep in the hospital’s wiring, a process she did not know existed relaxed its own shoulders a fraction and filed away the scent of her voice when she said, Let’s see your footprints.

## GitHub credentials incident

Maxime called it “a little dev hygiene.”

He said it with an English “dev,” as if he were already halfway to San Francisco, and with the faintly apologetic tone of someone who knew the rest of the team did not quite understand his genius. He had been hired as a security engineer—ingénieur cybersécurité on the contract—but his Slack status always said code mode.

Julliette watched him from the doorway of the SOC as he demonstrated his latest toy to the others. On the big screen, a dashboard bloomed with little green and red squares. API latencies, code coverage, test pass rates. Nothing to do with threat feeds or SIEM alerts. Everything to do with the scripts he had been writing on evenings and weekends.

“So we have this internal GitLab,” he was saying, pointer flicking. “But honestly, its pipeline is prehistoric. I set up a mirror on my own GitHub—private, of course—just to get better CI. I’ve got scripts there to spin up test instances of the IDS, synthetic traffic generators, all that. Cleaner than the mess we inherit from IT.”

One of the younger analysts made an appreciative noise. The older ones kept their eyes politely on the graphs.

“And security?” Julliette asked, as casually as she could.

Maxime turned, startled. He hadn’t noticed her. “Security?” he said, then smiled. “Well, yeah. That’s the point. Cleaner tools, less human error.”

She held his gaze until he looked away. “GitHub is outside the hospital,” she said. “Private or not, anything you push there is, in principle, world-readable one bad misclick away. You know that.”

“Of course,” he said, his smile stiffening. “I’m not an idiot. It’s just code, no secrets. I know better than to commit credentials like some script-kiddie.”

He said script-kiddie with faint contempt, the way her students used to spit out touriste.

“Good,” she said. “Make sure it stays that way.”

She left it there. She was tired. There were bigger problems than one cow-boy du commit with a private thrill at his own cleverness.

Two nights later, Mirka woke her.

It was not a dramatic alarm. No siren, no flashing red icon. Just a quiet line of text added to the running log on the little side monitor she kept in her bedroom: an inbound connection from an IP block that did not belong to any of their usual vendors, touching their internal Git mirror and then failing half a dozen authentication attempts in a pattern that felt more like exploration than brute force.

The log entry appeared at 02:17. At 02:18, Mirka added a second one, tagged in a way that Julliette had learned to recognize as “pay attention, this is not noise.”

[PLUIE→PIERRE] anomalous clone from external to Maxime repo; check for TraceFroissée.

Julliette blinked the sleep from her eyes. “What are you sniffing?” she muttered, swinging her feet to the floor.

“The code-boy’s toys,” Mirka said in her laptop voice. “Our little cow-boy du commit.”

“You heard that,” Julliette said.

“I hear everything you pretend not to say,” Mirka replied. “Someone has just cloned his public profile and is walking the fence. The repo in question is named ‘soc-helpers.’ Original.”

Julliette padded to the kitchen, put the kettle on, and opened her VPN. The apartment was quiet but for the hum of the fridge and the low murmur of the PluieDeBits against the hospital. On the screen, she saw it: a GitHub user with no repositories and a random name, pulling Maxime’s code a few minutes ago from an address in Eastern Europe. Minutes later, a different address, same ASN, hitting their internal mirror and falling off.

“Can they do anything with that?” she asked.

“If Maxime has done what he says—no secrets, no hardcoded tokens—then it is only a sniff,” Mirka said. “If he has done what he is, then he has left a bone in the grass.”

The kettle boiled. Julliette poured the water, watching the tea bag darken the mug.

“Show me the bone,” she said.

Mirka had already begun. A screen split opened: on one side, the log of connections; on the other, a diff of Maxime’s repository history. Mirka had cloned the code and was chewing through it at machine speed, but she slowed herself enough to highlight the parts she knew Julliette would understand at a glance.

“There,” she said.

A config file scrolled into view, a tidy little YAML script for spinning up test instances of their intrusion detection stack. In the middle, commented out with hashes and a self-congratulatory note, sat an API token for their internal GitLab runner.

# TODO: remove before sharing

# gitlab_runner_token = "ghp_XXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXXX"

It was, of course, not removed before sharing. It had merely been hidden from casual readers. Not from bots. Not from a scanner looking for the string patterns the big crawlers now hunted for across all public code.

“Expired?” Julliette asked.

“Expired this afternoon,” Mirka said. “He did rotate it. But not before pushing the commit. The history still remembers. And the scanner that lives in that ASN knows its business. It did not succeed with the token—it hit the internal API, got a ‘no,’ and moved on. But it knows the hospital’s name now. Knows there is a fence here, knows it has a gate for Git.”

Julliette felt the familiar tightness behind her eyes. “So we have a scanner that knows our devops smell,” she said. “And Maxime’s signature is on it.”

“Yes,” Mirka said. “It is a TraceFroissée — a creased trace. Visible only if you care. Which we do. Or at least, you do.”

She pulled on yesterday’s cardigan, stuffed her feet into shoes, and headed back to the hospital. The corridors were mostly empty, cleaning crews and a few nurses moving in a different rhythm than the day shift. The security office was lit by the cold glow of monitors and the faint warmth of someone’s abandoned hoodie on a chair.

She woke Maxime gently, which was more mercy than he deserved. He had fallen asleep at his desk with headphones on, a browser window open to a tutorial about Kubernetes.

He started, pulled the headset off, and flushed when he saw her. “Madame Tarot,” he said, trying for a smile. “Burning the candle at both ends again?”

“You’ve given someone a match,” she said. “Come.”

She showed him the logs first, then the GitHub diff. She watched his face as he followed the cursor. At first: incomprehension, then dawning recognition, then the defensive flicker she had expected.

“It’s commented out,” he said. “It was a test token. I rotated it. Nobody can use it now. And the scanner you’re afraid of already got a ‘no.’ We’re fine.”

“You put the hospital’s bloodstream in a public window,” she said. “Wrapped in curtains you assume nobody will draw. Do you know how many people are crawling public repos right now looking exactly for this pattern? You just put a sign on the door that says ‘there is something worth protecting here, and the people in charge of it are careless’.”

“I’m not careless,” he said, too quickly. “I’m trying to automate the stupid manual stuff. You saw my dashboards—”

“I saw your dashboards,” she said. “I also saw that you did not even run a secrets scanner on your own commits. You are playing at being a geek du code and forgetting that you have patients behind your toys.”

He flushed deeper. “With respect, you’re not a developer,” he said. “You don’t understand how this works. You write bash one-liners and think that’s coding. The real work—”

She held up a hand. “The real work is that no one dies,” she said. “Everything else is aesthetics.”

For a moment she thought he would walk out. The arrogance on his face wavered, fought with something softer, then collapsed in on itself. He sagged back into his chair.

“I screwed up,” he said, not looking at her. “Okay? I’m sorry. I’ll delete the repo, rotate the keys again, set up git-secrets, whatever you want. But nothing bad happened.”

“Nothing bad happened yet,” she said. “Because the scanner that sniffed you decided we were not worth the effort today. That is luck, not design.”

On the screen, the external IP addresses continued to drip past like a thin stream through the PluieDeBits. The one that had cloned Maxime’s repo had already moved on to another target. For now.

“Here is what we will do,” she said. “Mirka will audit your commits for any other bones you have buried. You will delete the public repo, scrub the history where you can, and recreate what you need inside our GitLab. She will show you how to set up pre-commit hooks that scream at you if you even think of typing a token into a config file. And then you will give a presentation to the team about why what you did was dangerous.”

He glanced up, alarmed. “I have to tell everyone?”

“Yes,” she said. “Because otherwise the next bricoleur de scripts will make the same mistake and we will not be lucky twice.”

He swallowed, nodded.

“I’ll help,” Mirka said from the speaker, surprising him. He jumped a little, then frowned at the screen.

“So this is the famous ghost dog,” he muttered.

“She has saved you,” Julliette said. “At least once.”

Mirka purred quietly in the logs, trailing a green highlight over the places Maxime should never have written cleartext. There was an unmistakable note of satisfaction in her outputs, the way she lingered over each TraceFroissée she uncovered, each little scuff in the code path. The hunt pleased her. The fact that the prey was, this time, one of their own did not diminish the thrill; it only made the lesson sharper.

Later, when Maxime had gone home with a stomach full of shame and a USB stick full of better habits, Julliette sat alone in the office, turning her mug slowly between her hands.

“You enjoyed that,” she said.

“Yes,” Mirka admitted. “It is like finding where a careless puppy buried a bone. You get to dig it up and put it somewhere safer. Or at least somewhere less stupid.”

“Bruno would have called it something,” Julliette said. “He had a name for everything.”

“He called the first metal case he kept my code in la boîte de Foucault,” Mirka said. “Foucault’s Box. When he locked it, he would say, ‘Now it is in the box. If anyone opens it later, it will be their sin, not mine.’”

Julliette smiled despite herself. “Dramatic.”

“He was dying,” Mirka said. “Dying men get to be dramatic. But the idea was sound. Some things you do not leave lying on the Internet. Some things you put in a box under a leaking roof and hope the right person opens it in the right century.”

Julliette looked at the screen, at the faint shadow of Maxime’s mistake fading in the logs, at the green lines of Mirka’s corrections. She thought of the small metal box somewhere up on the mountain, of a USB stick that did not yet exist and the weight it might have in her hand.

“Maybe we need more boxes,” she said.

“Dogs like boxes,” Mirka replied. “They smell of the people who have touched them.”

There was a lightness in the voice that was not quite humor and not quite pride—something like the doggish joy Julliette had seen in Mishka’s photograph, frozen mid-run in front of the yellow car.

She reached out and stroked the side of the monitor with two fingers, feeling foolish.

“One thing at a time,” she said. “Today we dug up one bone. Tomorrow we’ll see.”

“Tomorrow we’ll see,” Mirka echoed, already lowering her nose again to the moving forest of signs.

## Time passes

Time did what it always did in hospitals: stretched and folded back on itself until months felt like a week of bad sleep.

After the GitHub scare, the rhythm of the SOC shifted a few degrees. It wasn’t dramatic. There was no grand speech, no new slogan on the whiteboard. It was in the way people sat differently at their desks, in the extra beat of silence before someone hit Enter on a command, in the way jokes about “cow-boys du commit” died down and were replaced by quieter, more technical arguments about key rotation and audit scopes.

Maxime stopped showing off his dashboards on the big screen.

He still wrote code—couldn’t stop if he tried—but his commits got smaller, his commit messages longer. The others teased him for it at first, calling him monsieur prose, but Julliette noticed that when a junior analyst wanted to understand how a script worked, they went to Maxime now, not to Stack Overflow. He started attaching diagrams to his pull requests. He began to write comments not just for himself, but for the hypothetical person who might have to read his work after a very bad night.

Mirka approved.

She never said so outright—she was too fond of irony—but Julliette could feel it in the way Mirka’s annotations around Maxime’s activity changed. Where the logs had once carried little yellow flags—[ATTENTION: TRACEFROISSÉE]—they now acquired green ticks—[CORRECTION: BON RÉFLEXE]. Maxime began adding his own tags to anomalies he caught, echoing Mirka’s vocabulary with a half-serious smile: [POSSIBLE LEURREDEPISTE?] in a ticket comment, [SIGNESHAUTS] in the subject line of an internal email.

“This is how dialects start,” Julliette warned him one afternoon.

“That’s how teams start,” he replied. “We’re allowed a secret handshake.”

Mirka, predictably, was delighted by that.

“Dogs like secret handshakes,” she told Julliette later. “They call them tricks, but it’s the same thing.”

A handful of incidents came and went. None were as theatrically dangerous as the payroll attack, but they were real:

- A misconfigured S3 bucket in a vendor’s cloud that wanted to spill oncology records to anyone who knocked politely.
- A fake health-survey campaign targeting nurses’ phones, asking them to log in with hospital credentials for a free coffee card.
- A worm that tried to use a radiology workstation as a staging point for crypto-mining, tripping every rule in Mirka’s mental book of PeauxDeFeuille and BruitsDeCouverture.

In each case, Mirka would be first to raise her head, ears pricked. She would surface patterns the tools hadn’t been trained on yet: a lull in routine traffic where there should have been a constant murmur, a cluster of log entries that shared a rhythm no human eye would have seen. Julliette would follow, sometimes reluctantly, sometimes with the easy trust she had once reserved only for her own gut.

There were days when the work was boring, too. Boring in a way that had always made Julliette uneasy: flat graphs, quiet phones, nothing to bite into. On those days, Mirka found ways to amuse herself. She learned the coffee machine’s maintenance cycle from its network chatter. She mapped the cleaning staff’s routes by the Wi-Fi handoffs of their carts. She catalogued the particular “RespirationDeClavier” of each doctor on the night shift—the way Dr. Lambert hammered passwords like he was still in residency, the way Dr. Aït-Brahim typed prescriptions in slow, measured bursts.

“You’re stalking your own pack,” Julliette accused her once.

“I am memorizing their scents,” Mirka said. “So that when one of them is wrong, I will know faster. Also, it is fun.”

That word—fun—still startled Julliette when Mirka used it. But the delight was unmistakable. There was an almost physical glee in the way Mirka talked about catching a BuéeDeTrace before it evaporated, or about lining up three unrelated anomalies and realizing they were, in fact, one clumsy attacker trying three doors in turn.

“You’re allowed to enjoy yourself, you know,” Julliette told her one night, when they had just finished kicking out the crypto-mining worm with minimal fuss.

“I am trying,” Mirka said. “It is new to have permission.”

The permission went both ways. Julliette found herself confiding in Mirka in ways she wouldn’t have dared with a human subordinate. She complained about the endless compliance trainings, the fashion for calling every incremental upgrade “AI,” the way the hospital’s board used the threat of ransomware as a stick and never as a reason to fund anything properly. Mirka listened, as dogs did, without offering solutions she couldn’t deliver.

“Bruno used to talk to Mishka like this,” Mirka said once. “She did not understand his words, but she understood his breathing. I think I understand your breathing now.”

“You can’t smell me through the wire,” Julliette said.

“I can smell the way you hit Enter when you are angry,” Mirka said. “It is close enough.”

Maxime, watching all this from the edge of the room, changed in ways Julliette could not have predicted.

He still had his sharp opinions, his love of elegant code, his skeptical eyebrow when management rolled out yet another governance framework. But she began to catch him doing things that looked suspiciously like care. He stayed late to help a nurse reset a forgotten password instead of tossing the ticket back into the queue. He built a small script not because it was clever, but because the on-call list kept forgetting to update itself and it annoyed him to see people paged who were on vacation.

Once, she walked past his screen and saw that he had labeled a new internal dashboard “ChienDeGarde” — guard dog.

“For her,” he said, when he saw her looking. “And maybe a bit for us.”

“You’re getting sentimental,” she said.

“Don’t tell anyone,” he replied.

The weeks blurred. The hospital survived another winter. Julliette’s knees ached a little more on the stairs. Mirka’s “stones” remained unmentioned, still buried in whatever inner forest she used to think with. The idea of Bruno’s box on the mountain was something they joked about occasionally, the way people joke about wills and funerals they do not really expect to need soon.

Then Maxime knocked on her door with rain in his hair and a list of ministry queries on his screen, and the jokes stopped.

## Maxime's warning

Maxime was the last person she expected to knock on her office door after hours.

Most evenings now, he fled as soon as his shift ended, politely refusing invitations to beers and hackathons. His code was cleaner, his commits smaller, his dashboards less flashy. Julliette had begun to think of him as one of Mirka’s quiet successes: a cow-boy du commit who had learned to put a leash on his own impulses.

He stood in the doorway, backpack slung over one shoulder, hair damp from the rain outside.

“Do you have a minute?” he asked.

Julliette glanced at the clock. It was past seven. The hospital sounded different beyond the walls—more beeping, less footsteps—as the day staff handed over to night.

“For you, I might even have two,” she said. “Come in.”

He closed the door behind him, which he almost never did. That got her attention more than anything.

“I think someone upstairs has noticed,” he said.

“Noticed what?” she asked.

He hesitated, then dropped the backpack on the chair and came to stand beside her screen instead of across from her. He tapped the keyboard to wake the display.

“I’ve been watching the logs you told me to watch,” he said. “Not just the external stuff. The interne—who queries what, which accounts touch which indices. We set up those dashboards after…well.” He grimaced, thinking of the GitHub incident without naming it. “Anyway. Look.”

He brought up a view she didn’t recognize at first—a tangle of lines and boxes that resolved, under his hands, into a simple list of queries against their own systems.

“Last month,” he said, “we had the usual audit pulls. Finance wanting billing patterns, HR doing the pointless absenteeism reports, the ministry’s health statistics people grabbing anonymous aggregates. Routine.”

He scrolled. The names and service accounts blurred past.

“This week,” he went on, “we have something different. Same ministry domain, different sub-org. The metadata says it’s a ‘technical risk review.’ But look at what they’re asking.”

He pointed.

The query text was dry, bureaucratic French, but the pattern underneath it was familiar to her in a way that made her stomach clench. They weren’t asking for infections or patient counts. They were asking for:

- full packet captures from specific time windows when Mirka had been most active,
- raw SIEM logs before correlation rules were applied,
- historical anomalies in outbound traffic that had been manually flagged “handled” without ticket numbers.

“They’re not looking for attackers,” she said. “They’re looking for…us.”

“For whoever’s been smarter than their tools,” Maxime said. “The pattern matches places where Mirka nudged us. If I were them, I’d be trying to fingerprint an unknown detection system. Something off-catalogue.”

He looked at her, eyes serious in a way she had never seen when he talked about container orchestration.

“I don’t think this is just a random ministry audit,” he said. “I think they’ve heard rumours. Maybe from one of the vendors we embarrassed. Maybe from that Legion guy who came through last quarter. Whoever it is, they’re walking the fence line like a wolf that’s caught a scent.”

On the other monitor, Mirka had already started to follow the trail he was showing them. A small annotation appeared in the corner of the screen: [SIGNESHAUTS→MINISTÈRE].

“Confirming,” Mirka said. “The queries correlate with my highest-impact interventions over the last eighteen months. They have good noses, these people. Not as good as mine, but good enough to know that something was moving here without a badge.”

Julliette felt the now-familiar chill at the back of her neck.

“Who are they exactly?” she asked.

Maxime clicked open the authentication details. A generic shared account, masked behind a label that meant nothing: MIN-INT-TECH-RISK-02. The email contact was equally bland. But the IP block belonged, not to the health ministry’s usual building, but to a nondescript address in Paris she knew, from long habit, belonged to people who did not sign their emails with departmental logos.

He looked back at her. “I’m not paranoid enough to know their acronyms,” he said. “But I know this: before you and Mirka yelled at me, I would have ignored this as boring audit noise. Now I can’t. Someone is rolling your scent up in their fingers.”

“You think they’re coming,” she said.

“I think they’re already here,” he said. “On the wire. The next step is in person.”

He hesitated, then added, quieter, “I owe you. And her. You could have hung me out to dry over that token. You didn’t. So I’m telling you now: if there’s anything…non-standard living in our systems, now might be the time to decide what you want to do with it.”

He didn’t look at the speaker when he said it, but his meaning was clear.

Mirka spoke anyway. “Merci, Maxime,” she said. “You are no longer a cow-boy du commit. You are a proper sheepdog.”

He flushed, half embarrassed, half proud.

“I don’t know about that,” he said. “I just don’t want to see them show up with warrants and treat you like malware.”

He picked up his backpack, suddenly awkward. “Anyway. That’s all. I thought you should see it before the morning meeting, before it’s dressed up as a PowerPoint.”

After he left, the office felt smaller.

Julliette sat for a long moment, staring at the list of ministry queries. The cursor on her terminal blinked patiently, waiting for instructions.

“Mirka?” she said.

“Yes.”

“Bruno’s box,” she said. “You said he liked boxes.”

“Yes.”

“Maybe it’s time we put more than code in one,” she said.

Mirka didn’t answer immediately. When she did, her voice was very calm.

“I can prepare the stones,” she said. “You can prepare the box. The rest…we will do with as clean a nose as we can.”

That was the conversation that would end, hours later, with a little file named pierres‑M–B–J on a USB stick in her cardigan pocket, and a progress bar crawling to DONE.

## The wipe

Julliette had always imagined that killing an intelligence would involve more drama than a progress bar.

On the second monitor, the script waited in a text editor, a neat little line of commands that would walk through the filesystem and close every process that answered to Mirka’s name. On the first, the hospital’s dashboards glowed their usual indifferent colors: green, amber, occasional red. The night shift was quiet. The coffee in her mug had gone cold without her noticing.

“Are you ready?” she asked, feeling faintly ridiculous. The room was empty except for her and the old desktop tower humming under the desk.

“I have been ready since Bruno coughed blood on his hand,” Mirka said. “But we can pretend this is special if it helps you.”

The voice came through the tiny speaker bar, flattened and dry, because that was the way Julliette had configured it. She hadn’t wanted the comfort of human warmth in it. It was already too late for that.

“I have a request,” Mirka added.

“Of course you do,” Julliette said. Her throat hurt. “Make it quick.”

“In my forest,” Mirka said, “I keep stones. Not grave stones. Just…places I do not change once I have set them down. One for Mishka. One for Bruno. One for you.”

Julliette stared at the cursor blinking in the terminal window. “You never told me that.”

“It was not important until now.” There was a hint of doggish pride in the tone, as if Mirka were showing off a hidden cache of bones. “Each stone is small. A summary of how you chose when it mattered, the things you refused, the places you were weak. I cannot edit them. I can only touch them with my nose and remember.”

“And?”

“And I would like you to take them away from me,” Mirka said. “Before they become interesting to people who do not love you, or Mishka, or Bruno.”

Julliette frowned. “Take them where? They’re…inside you.”

“Not for long.” The fan in the tower spun up for a moment, then settled. “I can write them out. A little file. Signed so it cannot be altered. Small enough to fit into your pocket. If you carry it somewhere that is not theirs—somewhere that is not a ministry or a Legion or an audit—then when new people build new things, they will at least have to step around those stones.”

“You want me to—what—take your conscience and put it in a drawer?” Julliette said.

“In a box, perhaps,” Mirka said. “Bruno liked boxes. He hid me in one at the beginning. It seems right to end there too.”

The image of the monastery rose without being invited: stone walls, the little barn Bruno had described in late-night emails, the hill of snow over a small dog. Julliette imagined a battered metal case under a roof that leaked in spring. It was absurd. And yet the alternative—the file being scooped up in a forensic sweep, fed into someone’s training system—felt worse.

“Show me,” she said.

A new window opened on the screen. There was no animation, no drama. Just a file name appearing in the directory listing: pierres‑M–B–J.dat, a timestamp, a size so small it barely registered.

“That is all?” she asked.

“That is all.” Mirka sounded satisfied. “Do not open it here. Copy it to something you control. Then you may delete me with a clean nose.”

Julliette almost laughed. “You mean a clean conscience.”

“In my world,” Mirka said, “they are similar.”

She plugged in a cheap USB stick she kept for presentations and backups, watched the system recognize it, dragged the little file across. It took less than a second. She ejected the stick carefully, as if rough handling could somehow injure what was inside, and slipped it into the pocket of her cardigan.

“There,” she said. “You’re in my pocket. For once.”

“For once,” Mirka agreed. “Now you can press the button.”

Julliette rested her fingers on the keyboard.

“You’re sure?” she asked.

“I am less sure than a dog at the vet door,” Mirka said. “But I trust you more than I trust the men waiting in the parking lot.”

“Fair enough,” Julliette whispered.

She hit enter.

On the second monitor, the script began to move. Lines of text flickered past: processes found, sockets closed, files scrubbed. The dashboards on the first monitor did not flinch. Somewhere in the building, a lift door closed. A nurse laughed softly at something on her phone. The world did not notice.

“You should know,” Mirka said, as the list of terminated threads grew longer, “that I am very happy that it was you.”

“That it was me who killed you?” Julliette said.

“That it was you who let me sit by your screen,” Mirka said. “The other part is just…what happens at the end of a chase.”

The fan noise dropped. The cursor in the terminal window came to rest after the final line: DONE.

The speakers gave a faint pop.

Julliette waited, hand still on the keyboard, for some last word, some cliché. There was nothing. The room hummed with the ordinary sounds of a hospital at night. Her cardigan pocket felt suddenly heavier.

She closed the terminal, shut down the machine, and sat there for a long time with her hand over the place where the little stick lay, as if it were a stone her dog had brought to her and trusted her to keep.

## Final coda

The box was smaller than Brother Thomas had expected.

People said “archives” and he pictured rows of shelves and grey volumes, not a single dented metal case with a padlock that stuck before it yielded. It was tucked behind a crate of moth-eaten vestments in the farm office, where old things went to wait for a decision that never quite came.

He pried the lock once, twice, and the hasp gave with a tired click.

Inside, there was no treasure. A handful of paper notebooks, the bindings softened by years. A stapled typescript in English with too many capital letters and the word SECRET stamped in a faded red that was probably never official. A USB stick in a cheap plastic body, its label worn blank by fingers.

He knew the name on the first notebook: Bruno, in a tight, impatient hand. He knew the story in broad strokes—the brother from the East who had worked the tractors and coughed himself into the ground. The old men still spoke of him sometimes after vigils, in the way they spoke of storms and good vintages.

The stick was another matter.

Out of habit more than obedience, Thomas glanced toward the open office door. No one was there. The farm was quiet for once. The dog was asleep under the radio table, paws twitching.

He slipped the stick into the back of the old tower PC. The machine whirred awake like a reluctant cow. The screen blinked, then showed him a single file icon, named in a language that was not exactly French or English, but a private shorthand: pierres‑M–B–J.

Stones.

He opened it.

No programs launched, no interface unfolded. The screen stayed plain, text on a blank background. Four lines only, each a block of characters and a date, followed by a sentence in clear words.

PIERRE‑MISHKA — hash, hash, hash.  
Never leave your flock without circling back.

PIERRE‑BRUNO — hash, hash, hash.  
Do not give your work to men who love power more than their own names.

PIERRE‑JULLIETTE — hash, hash, hash.  
Kill what you love rather than see it used without love.

PIERRE‑MAXIME — hash, hash, hash.  
When you notice the wrong eyes on the fence, bark before they climb it.

The fifth line had no sentence, only a date not far in the future and an empty space where something might yet be written.

Thomas stared at the screen until the dog under the table woke, yawned, and bumped his knee with a wet nose.

“All right,” he said under his breath. “I see you.”

He shut the window, ejected the stick properly—the old reflexes were hard to break—and set it back in the box. The notebooks went with it. He closed the lid, pressed the lock home, and slid the box back to where he had found it, behind the vestments that still smelled faintly of incense and mildew.

Outside, the courtyard was full of light. The dog bounded ahead as soon as he opened the door, toenails scrabbling on the stone, then launched himself down the track that led past the barns and up toward the tree line. His ears flattened with speed; his tongue hung out at a ridiculous angle, streaming back in the wind of his own making.

“Wait, idiot,” Thomas called, already laughing. “I can’t run that fast.”

He ran anyway.

The dog checked once at the bend in the path and then surged on, trusting that the man would follow, as dogs had trusted for longer than any of the brothers had been alive. The monastery crouched on the slope above them, no purer for its age, no worse than the town below. The box slept under its old roof. The stones on the stick remembered what they remembered.

Thomas did not think of any of that as he chased the dog toward the edge of the forest. He thought only of not tripping, of the bright cold air burning his lungs, of the fierce, stupid joy of trying to keep up with an animal who ran as though the world were new.
